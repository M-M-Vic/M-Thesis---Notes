\subsection{Probability Generating Functions (PGF)}

There exist several approaches when it comes to obtaining the long-run state space distribution of the system. For simple models such as the classic $M/M/1$-queue, there are several very illustrative ways to obtain it. In \cite{adan2002queueing} they mention a direct approach in which they solve a quadratic equation, an approach involving a recursion in the transition probabilities, and a Probability Generating Function (PGF) approach. The latter is more interesting for us, not only because our model is already more complex than the $M/M/1$-queue but also because the goal of this project is to study models that are far more complex than the one that this section addresses. We hope that the methods and arguments involved in the PGF approach  provide the foundation to study a more complex system: be it with multiple servers, abandonment rates, customers with "impatience", or all those at once.


\subsubsection{Definition}

In order to derive the PGF, we must first provide a definition for it:
\begin{equation}
       P(x,y) = \mathbb{E}[x^{N_1} y^{N_2}] = \sum_{n_1=0}^{\infty} \sum_{n_2=0}^{\infty} \pi(n_1,n_2) x^{n_1} y^{n_2}. \\
\end{equation}
Furthermore, we must define some terms that will appear in the derivation. These functions correspond to the dynamics that appear at the boundaries. This is when one of the queues is empty, conditional on the fact that there is already someone in service:
\begin{align}
   P_x(x) = \sum_{n_1=0}^{\infty} \pi(n_1,0) x^{n_1} \\
   P_y(y) = \sum_{n_2=0}^{\infty} \pi(0,n_2) y^{n_2} \\
\end{align}
Another term that will appear once we proceed to use the balance equations is the following. However, this will just be an intermediate step towards the final result, as we will see, it will be possible to express it on other terms:
\begin{equation}
    P_y(y |n_1=1) = \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} \pi(1,n_2) y^{n_2}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{$M/M/1$}{M/M/1} queue}

The $M/M/1$ queue is one of the most studied queueing systems. There is a great deal of literature available for this type of queue, but the content of this subsection will be taken from the textbook \cite{adan2002queueing}.

It consists of a Poisson arrival process with rate $\lambda$ and service times being exponentially distributed with rate $\mu$, where there is $1$ server. This queueing system will be stable if and only if $\rho = \frac{\lambda}{\mu} < 1$. This quantity is known as the system's \textit{load}, as it's the fraction of time the server is working.

Having $\rho < 1$ gives us the possibility of studying the long-term behavior of the system, as it ensures that a limiting distribution is in place: let $L=0,1,2,\dots$  be the number of customers in the system and define $\pi_n$ to be the limiting probability of finding $n$ customers in the system at a random moment in time. We will then have
\begin{equation}
    \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \pi_n = 1.
\end{equation}

The resulting system is the so-called birth-death process and it is equivalent to a Markov chain where the balance equations are as follows:
\begin{align}
    &0 = -\lambda \pi_0 + \mu \pi_1  \label{eq:mm1_balance1}\\
    &0 = \lambda \pi_{n-1} - (\lambda+\mu) \pi_n + \mu \pi_{n+1} & \text{for} \quad n \geq 1 \label{eq:mm1_balance2}
\end{align}

In \cite{adan2002queueing}, the formula for $\pi_0$ is derived in various ways: one with a direct approach, one that involves a recursion, and one involving PGF's. Since this document tries to derive PGF's for more complex systems, the latter one will be best suited as an introduction.

Let $L(z)$ be the Laplace-Stiltjes Transform (LST) of the number of customers in the system, i.e., for $|z| \leq 1$ we have:
\begin{equation}
    L(z) = \mathbb{E}[z^L]=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \pi_n z^n.
\end{equation}

Multiplying the $n$th balance equations (in Equations \eqref{eq:mm1_balance1} and \eqref{eq:mm1_balance2} by $z^n$ and summing them up all over $n$, we will obtain 
\begin{equation*}
    0 = \mu \pi_0 (1 - z^{-1}) + (\lambda z + \mu z^{-1} - (\lambda + \mu)) L(z),
\end{equation*}
which yields the following solution:
\begin{equation}
    L(z) = \frac{\pi_0}{1 - \rho z} = \frac{1 - \rho}{1 - \rho z} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} (1 - \rho) \rho^n z^n,
\end{equation}

Here, they use the fact that $L(1)=1$ to determine $\pi_0=1-\rho$ and obtain
\begin{equation*}
    \pi_n = (1 - \rho) \rho^n \quad \text{for} \quad n \geq 0.
\end{equation*}

Among the objects we can measure in the $M/M/1$ queueing system is the so-called \textit{busy period}, denoted by $BP$, which is the time interval starting when a customer arrives to an idle system and ending once the system becomes empty again: during that time, a customer is in service, and customers may or may not arrive before that service completion. The Laplace-Stieltjes Transform and expectation of the busy period are well-known:
\begin{align}
    \widetilde{BP}(s) &= \frac{(\mu + \lambda +s) - \sqrt{(\mu + \lambda + s)^2 - 4 \mu \lambda}}{2 \lambda} 
    \label{eq:bp_mm1}\\
    \mathbb{E}\left[ BP\right] &= \frac{1/ \mu}{1 - \rho}. \label{eq:bp_expected_mm1}
\end{align}
The transform is known to be invertible, so the distribution of $BP$ is also known. However, that is beyond the scope of this document, as it involves a more complex mathematical object called \textit{modified Bessel functions}.

\subsection{Multiple Adaptive Vacation model}

In \cite{tian2006vacation}, the authors study the so-called Multiple Vacation Models. A result is given for the distribution of the number of customers in the queue ($L_v$), where it behaves as the sum of two independent random variables $L$ and $L_d$, where $L$ refers to that of the classic models $M/G/1$ and $L_d$ accounts for the delay caused by the vacation effect. It reads like this:
\begin{equation*}
    L_v = L+ L_d.
\end{equation*}
Naturally, the PGF of that is the following:
\begin{equation}
    L_v(z) = \mathbb{E}\left[ z^{L_v} \right]
    = \mathbb{E}\left[ z^{L + L_d} \right]
    = \mathbb{E}\left[ z^{L} \right] \mathbb{E}\left[ z^{L_d} \right] = L(z) L_d(z).
\end{equation}

In their book, they provide closed formulas for both expressions, being as follows:
\begin{align*}
    L(z) = \frac{(1-\rho) (1-z) \widetilde{B}(\lambda(1-z))}{\widetilde{B}(\lambda(1-z)) - z}
     \quad \text{and} \quad
     L_d(z) = \frac{1 - \widetilde{B}(\lambda(1-z))}{ \lambda_2\mathbb{E}\left[V\right] (1-z)},
\end{align*}

Here, $\rho=\lambda \mathbb{E}[B]$ is the lead of the system and is not necessarily equal to $\frac{\lambda}{\mu}$. Thus:
\begin{equation}
L(z) = \frac{(1-\lambda \mathbb{E}[B]) \widetilde{B}(\lambda(1-z)) \left[ 1 - \widetilde{B}(\lambda(1-z)) \right]}{\lambda \mathbb{E}[V] \left[ \widetilde{B}(\lambda(1-z)) - z \right]}
\end{equation}

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{$M/M/1+M$}{M/M/1+M} queue}

\section{Model description: Model B}

\textit{Note: This section defines a general queueing system with jockeying (Model B). A baseline variant without jockeying (Model A) is simply a special case where $\gamma_1 = \gamma_2 = 0$. Knowing that, it suffices to explain Model B.}

The model discussed in this section $M/M/1$ queueing system with $2$ priority classes, where customers are served on a First-Come, First-Served (FCFS) within their own class and those coming from the priority class (Class-$1$) have non-preemptive priority over those of the non-priority class (Class-$2$), meaning that, when a Class-$2$ customer is in service, that service will not be interrupted by a potential arrival of a Class-$1$ customer. Moreover, we will take into account jockeying, i.e. customers abandoning their queue and moving to the other queue at a rate that depends on the amount of customers in the queue.

This model, being an $M/M/1$ queueing model according to Kendall's notation, features arrivals driven by a Poisson process with arrival rates $\lambda_i$ for Class-$i$, exponentially distributed service times with rate $\mu$, and service capacity of $1$ customer at a time. In addition, customers departing from queue $i$ will move to the other queue at a per-customer rate of $\gamma_i$. 

In order to define the stochastic processes that drive this queueing system, it is necessary to introduce the following random variables:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathcal{I}:$ binary indicator for the server being idle or busy $\mathcal{I}\in \mathbb{B}$
    \item $N_1, N_2:$ number of customers of class $1$ and $2$, respectively, in their respective queues
    \item $N:$ total number of customers in the queues;
\end{itemize}
Note that the variables $N_1$, $N_2$ and $N$ count the number of customers in the queues, as opposed to the customers in the system. This definition is convenient given that we have a common service rate for both priority classes, and hence the states of the system are equivalent regardless of the priority class the customer in service comes from. Since having customers in line directly implies that someone is in service, we will denote the idle state as $(0)$ and the rest of the states as the number of people in each queue as the tuple $(n_1,n_2)$, where $n_i$ denotes the number of customers in the queue for class-$i$. This is the most conventional way to define the state space, namely $S$ in such a queueing system. However, we can also look at the total number of customers in the queues, which behaves like a regular $M/M/1$ queueing system and is driven by a much more studied and simple set of dynamics. We can define an alternative state space $\widetilde{S}$ looking at the idle state $(0)$ and the pair $(n_2,n)$ —we could also choose $(n_1,n)$—, which already gives that we will have $(n-n_2)$ Class-$1$ customers in the priority queue. Note that, since $n$ is the total number of customers, we need to add the constraint $n \geq n_2$.
Therefore, we define our state spaces as follows:
\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
        &S := \{(0)\} \cup \{ (n_1,n_2) \mid n_1,n_2 \geq 0 \},
        \label{eq:state_space_s}
    \end{split} \\
    \begin{split}
        &\widetilde{S} := \{(0)\} \cup \{ (n_2,n) \mid n \geq n_2 \geq 0 \}.
        \label{eq:state_space_stilde}
    \end{split}    
\end{align}

For these state spaces, we define the limiting —as opposed to time-dependent— probabilities of finding the system in that state in the long run as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\pi_0$ for the idle state $(0)$, which belongs to both spaces $S$ and $\widetilde{S}$;
    \item $\pi(n_1,n_2)$ for states $(n_1,n_2) \in S$, where $n_1,n_2 \geq 0$;
    \item $\widetilde{\pi}(n_2,n)$ for states $(n_2,n) \in \widetilde{S}$, where $n \geq n_2 \geq 0$.
\end{itemize}


Regarding the parameters of the system, the arrival process for each class follows a Poisson process (i.e., the interarrival times are exponentially distributed). By the properties of Poisson processes, the total number of arrivals will also form a Poisson process. Furthermore, we have a common service rate for exponentially distributed service times. We can organize our notation into class-specific parameters (alongside our common service rate) and class-blind totals. 
For the class-specific metrics and service rate, we define:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\lambda_1,\lambda_2$: arrival rates of classes $1$ and $2$, respectively;
    \item $\mu$: service rate of a customer, regardless of their priority class;
    \item $\rho_i=\frac{\lambda_i}{\mu}$: system load of class $i$, for $i\in\{1,2\}$;
    \item $\gamma_1,\gamma_2$: per-customer jockeying rates departing from the queues of class $1$ and $2$, respectively.
\end{itemize}
For the aggregate system parameters, we define:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\lambda=\lambda_1+\lambda_2$: total arrival rate;
    \item $\rho=\frac{\lambda}{\mu}$: total system load.
\end{itemize}

For stability conditions, we need the system to be able to keep up, on average, with the workload it was assigned. It suffices to set $\rho=\frac{\lambda_1+\lambda_2}{\mu}<1$.

Since the service times are the same for both classes, the total number of customers will behave like a classic $M/M/1$-queue with parameters $\lambda=\lambda_1+\lambda_2$ and $\mu$, it is well known for such a system that the idle —limiting— probability is as follows:
\begin{align*}
    \pi_0 = 1-\rho = 1 - \frac{\lambda_1+\lambda_2}{\mu}
\end{align*}
And, for our system, this has the following implication:
\begin{equation*}
    \sum_{n_1=0}^{\infty}\sum_{n_2=0}^{\infty} \pi(n_1,n_2) = 1 - \pi_0 = 1 - (1-\rho)=\rho=\frac{\lambda_1+\lambda_2}{\mu},
\end{equation*}
which will be relevant in our derivations, as we will look at the queue length conditioned on the server being busy.

We also have enough information to derive $\pi(0,0)$. This corresponds to the probability of having exactly $1$ customer in the system (the server is busy, but the queues are empty)—regardless of their class, due to the common service time distribution. This holds for both state spaces $S$ and $\widetilde{S}$:
\begin{equation}
    \pi(0,0) = \tpi(0,0) = (1-\rho)\rho.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Derivation of equations for (Partial) Probability Generating functions}

In order to study the limiting behaviour of this two-class system, we define the bivariate Probability Generating Function (PGF) of the joint number of customers in the queue for each class, $N_1$ and $N_2$:
\begin{equation}
    P(x,y) = \mathbb{E}[x^{N_1} y^{N_2}] = \sum_{n_1=0}^{\infty} \sum_{n_2=0}^{\infty} \pi(n_1,n_2) x^{n_1} y^{n_2}.
\end{equation}

The derivation that will be carried out here includes the boundary terms, where at least one of the two queues is empty:
\begin{align}
    P_x(x) &= P(x,0) = \sum_{n_1=0}^{\infty} \pi(n_1,0) x^{n_1} \\
    P_y(y) &= P(0,y) = \sum_{n_2=0}^{\infty} \pi(0,n_2) y^{n_2}.
\end{align}

Note that both of these terms include the state where the server is busy but both queues are empty, which is represented by $\pi(0,0)$, while the state where the entire system is empty (the idle state) is treated separately.


Another term that arises during the application of the balance equations characterizes the boundary dynamics when the Class-$1$ contains exactly one customer:
\begin{equation}
    P_1(y) = \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} \pi(1,n_2) y^{n_2}.
\end{equation}
This term serves as an intermediate step; as the derivation proceeds, it will be possible to express it in terms of the primary boundary functions $P_x(x)$ and $P_y(y)$.

In the same fashion, we introduce the concept of the \textbf{Partial Probability Generating Function (PPGF)}. This technique, notably utilized by Cohen \cite{Cohen1950}, consists of transforming only a subset of the random variables while maintaining the discrete indices of the remainder. In the context of a priority queue, this method is particularly advantageous: it allows us to exploit the well-known properties of the priority class (often a standard $M/M/1$ system) and focus the analytical effort on the non-trivial PGF of the non-priority class.

While Cohen originally developed this framework for multi-server systems, we apply it here to the single-server case. To avoid the potential confusion stemming from mid-20th-century notation, we provide a modern formulation consistent with the variables defined above.

We define the PPGF with respect to the second variable $N_2$, conditioned on the first queue being in state $n_1$, as:
\begin{equation}
    \widetilde{P}(n_1, y) = \mathbb{E} \left[y^{N_2} \mathbb{1}_{\{ N_1=n_1\}} \right] = \sum_{n_2=0}^{\infty} \pi(n_1,n_2) y^{n_2}.
\end{equation}

Similarly, the transform with respect to the first variable $N_1$, while maintaining the discrete state $n_2$ for the second queue, is defined as:
\begin{equation}
    \widetilde{P}(x, n_2) = \mathbb{E} \left[x^{N_1} \mathbb{1}_{\{ N_2=n_2\}} \right] = \sum_{n_1=0}^{\infty} \pi(n_1,n_2) x^{n_1}.
\end{equation}

These partial transforms are linked to the joint PGF by the following relations:
\begin{equation}
    P(x,y) = \sum_{n_1=0}^{\infty} \widetilde{P}(n_1, y) x^{n_1} = \sum_{n_2=0}^{\infty} \widetilde{P}(x, n_2) y^{n_2}.
\end{equation}

    \begin{split}
        &(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu ) \sum_{n_1=1}^{\infty} \pi(n_1,0) x^{n_1}
        + \gamma_1 x \sum_{n_1=1}^{\infty} n_1 \pi(n_1,0) x^{n_1-1} \\
        & \quad = \quad \lambda_1 x  \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \pi(m,0) x^{m}
        + \mu x^{-1}  \sum_{m=2}^{\infty} \pi(m,0) x^{m}
        + \gamma_2 x  \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \pi(m,1) x^{m}
    \end{split} \nonumber \\
    \begin{split}
        &(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu )\left[ \sum_{n_1=0}^{\infty} \pi(n_1,0) x^{n_1} - \pi(0,0)\right]
        + \gamma_1 x \sum_{n_1=0}^{\infty} n_1 \pi(n_1,0) x^{n_1-1} \\
        & \quad = \quad \lambda_1 x  \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \pi(m,0) x^{m} \\
        & \quad \quad + \mu x^{-1} \left[  \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \pi(m,0) x^{m}- x \pi(1,0) - \pi(0,0)\right] \\
        & \quad \quad+ \gamma_2 x  \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \pi(m,1) x^{m}
    \end{split}\nonumber \\
    \begin{split}
        &(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu )\left[ P_x(x) - \pi(0,0)\right]
        + \gamma_1 x \frac{d}{dx} P_x(x) \\
        & \quad = \quad \lambda_1 x  P_x(x) \\
        & \quad \quad + \mu x^{-1} \left[  P_x(x)- x \pi(1,0) - \pi(0,0)\right] \\
        & \quad \quad+ \gamma_2 x  P_x(x | n_2=1)
    \end{split} \nonumber \\
    \begin{split}
        & \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) - \mu x^{-1} - \lambda_1 x \right] P_x(x)
        + \gamma_1 x \frac{d}{dx} P_x(x) \\
        & \quad = \quad \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) - \mu x^{-1} \right] \pi(0,0)
        - \mu \pi(1,0) + \gamma_2 x P_x(x|n_2=1)
    \end{split}\nonumber  \\
    \begin{split}
        & \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu)xy - \mu y - \lambda_1 x^2 y \right] P_x(x)
        + \gamma_1 x^2 y \frac{d}{dx} P_x(x) \\
        & \quad = \quad \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu)xy - \mu y \right] \pi(0,0)
        - \mu xy \pi(1,0) + \gamma_2 x^2 y P_x(x|n_2=1)
    \end{split} \label{eq:n1n2_transform_x-boundary_relation}
\end{align}

Again, we replicate the procedure for the $y$-axis balance equation:
\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
        & \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} [\lambda_1+\lambda_2+\mu + \gamma_2 n_2] \pi(0,n_2)y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad = \quad \lambda_2 \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} \pi(0,n_2-1) y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad \quad + \mu \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} \pi(1,n_2) y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad \quad + \mu \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} \pi(0,n_2+1) y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad \quad + \gamma_1 \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} \pi(1,n_2-1) y^{n_2}
    \end{split} \nonumber \\
    \begin{split}
        &(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} \pi(0,n_2) y^{n_2}\\ 
        &+ \gamma_2 y \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} n_2 \pi(0,n_2) y^{n_2-1} \\
        & \quad = \quad \lambda_2 y \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} \pi(0,n_2-1) y^{n_2-1} \\
        & \quad \quad + \mu \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} \pi(1,n_2) y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad \quad + \mu y^{-1}\sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} \pi(0,n_2+1) y^{n_2+1} \\
        & \quad \quad + \gamma_1 y \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} \pi(1,n_2-1) y^{n_2-1}
    \end{split}  \nonumber \\
    \begin{split}
        &(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} \pi(0,n_2) y^{n_2}\\ 
        &+ \gamma_2 y \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} n_2 \pi(0,n_2) y^{n_2-1} \\
        & \quad = \quad \lambda_2 y \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \pi(0,m) y^{m} \\
        & \quad \quad + \mu \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} \pi(1,n_2) y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad \quad + \mu y^{-1} \sum_{m=2}^{\infty} \pi(0,m) y^m \\
        & \quad \quad + \gamma_1 y \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \pi(1,m) y^m
    \end{split} \nonumber  \\
    \begin{split}
        & (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) \left[ \sum_{n_2=0}^{\infty} \pi(0,n_2) y^{n_2} - \pi(0,0)\right] \\
        & + \gamma_2 y \left[ \sum_{n_2=0}^{\infty} n_2 \pi(0,n_2) y^{n_2-1} \right] \\
        & \quad = \quad \lambda_2 y \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \pi(0,m) y^m \\
        & \quad \quad + \mu \left[ \sum_{n_2=0}^{\infty} \pi(1,n_2) y^{n_2} - \pi(1,0) \right] \\
        & \quad \quad + \mu y^{-1} \left[ \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \pi(0,m) y^m - y \pi(0,1) - \pi(0,0) \right] \\
        & \quad \quad + \gamma_1 y \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \pi(1,m) y^m
    \end{split} \nonumber  \\
    \begin{split}
        &(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) \left[ P_y(y) - \pi(0,0)\right] + \gamma_2 y \frac{d}{d y} P_y(y) \\
        & \quad = \quad \lambda_2 y P_y(y) \\
        & \quad \quad+ \mu \left[ P_y(y | n_1=1) - \pi(1,0) \right] \\
        & \quad \quad + \mu y^{-1} \left[ P_y(y) - y \pi(0,1) - \pi(0,0) \right] \\
        & \quad \quad + \gamma_1 y P_y(y| n_1=1)
    \end{split} \nonumber  \\
    \begin{split}
        &\left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) - \mu y^{-1} - \lambda_2 y \right] P_y(y)
        + \gamma_2 y \frac{d}{dy} P_y(y) \\
        & \quad = \quad \left[ \mu + \gamma_1 y \right] P_y(y|n_1=1)  - \mu \pi(1,0) - \mu \pi(0,1) + \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) - \mu y^{-1} \right] \pi(0,0)
    \end{split} \nonumber  \\
    \begin{split}
        &\left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) - \mu y^{-1} - \lambda_2 y \right] P_y(y)
        + \gamma_2 y \frac{d}{dy} P_y(y) \\
        & \quad = \quad \left[ \mu + \gamma_1 y \right] P_y(y|n_1=1)+ \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) - \mu y^{-1} - (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)\right] \pi(0,0)
    \end{split} \nonumber  \\
    \begin{split}
        &\left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) - \mu y^{-1} - \lambda_2 y \right] P_y(y)
        + \gamma_2 y \frac{d}{dy} P_y(y) \\
        & \quad = \quad \left[ \mu + \gamma_1 y \right] P_y(y|n_1=1)+ \left[  \mu - \mu y^{-1} \right] \pi(0,0)
    \end{split}  \nonumber \\
    \begin{split}
        &\left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu)xy - \mu x - \lambda_2 x y^2 \right] P_y(y)
        + \gamma_2 x y^2 \frac{d}{dy} P_y(y) \\
        & \quad = \quad \left[ (\mu + \gamma_1 y)xy \right] P_y(y|n_1=1) - \left[  \mu x (1-y) \right] \pi(0,0)
    \end{split} \label{eq:n1n2_transform_y-boundary_relation}
\end{align}

We can now combine Equation \eqref{eq:n1n2_transform_intertior_relation} and Equation \eqref{eq:n1n2_transform_x-boundary_relation}, where many terms cancel out, leaving us with the following, remarkably cleaner, expression:
\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
        &\left[ (\lambda_1+\lambda_2+ \mu)xy - \mu y - \lambda_1 x^2 y - \lambda_2 x y^2 \right] P(x,y) \\
        & +\left[ \gamma_1 xy(x-y) \right] \frac{\partial}{\partial x} P(x,y) \\
        & +\left[ \gamma_2 xy(y-x)\right] \frac{\partial}{\partial y} P(x,y) \\
        & \quad = \quad \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu)xy - \mu y - \lambda_2 x y^2 \right] P_y(y) \\
        & \quad \quad + \left[ -\mu xy -\gamma_1 x y^2 \right] P_y(y | n_1=1) \\
        & \quad \quad + \gamma_2 x y^2 \frac{d}{dy} P_y(y)
    \end{split} \label{eq:n1n2_plugged_interior_and_x}
\end{align}
Now, isolating $P_y(y|n_1=1)$ in Equation \eqref{eq:n1n2_transform_y-boundary_relation} and plugging it into Equation \eqref{eq:n1n2_plugged_interior_and_x} makes a cascade of cancelations that result in:
\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
        &\left[ (\lambda_1+\lambda_2+ \mu)xy - \mu y - \lambda_1 x^2 y - \lambda_2 x y^2 \right] P(x,y) \\
        &+ \left[ \gamma_1 xy(x-y) \right] \frac{\partial}{\partial x} P(x,y) \\
        &+ \left[ \gamma_2 xy(y-x)\right] \frac{\partial}{\partial y} P(x,y) \\
        &\quad = \quad \mu(x-y) P_y(y) \\
        & \quad\quad- \mu x(1-y) \pi(0,0)
    \end{split} \nonumber \\
    \begin{split}
        &\left[ (\lambda_1+\lambda_2+ \mu)xy - \mu y - \lambda_1 x^2 y - \lambda_2 x y^2 \right] P(x,y) 
        + xy(x-y) \left[ \gamma_1 \frac{\partial}{\partial x} P(x,y) - \gamma_2 \frac{\partial}{\partial y} P(x,y) \right] \\
        &\quad = \quad \mu(x-y) P_y(y) 
         - \mu x(1-y) \pi(0,0)
    \end{split} \label{eq:n1n2_model-b_fundamental_equation}.
\end{align}


\subsubsection{Equation for PPGF in state space $\widetilde{S}$}

We start by taking our interior balance equation (Equation \eqref{eq:n1n_balance_interior}), multiplying by $y^{n_2}$ and adding up throughout its domain (i.e., $1 \leq n_2 \leq n-1, n_2 \leq n-1$):

\begin{align*}
    \begin{split}
        & (\lambda_1+\lambda_2+\mu) \sum_{n_2=1}^{n-1} \tpi(n_2,n) y^{n_2} \\
        & + \gamma_1 \sum_{n_2=1}^{n-1} (n-n_2) \tpi(n_2,n)y^{n_2} \\
        & + \gamma_2 \sum_{n_2=1}^{n-1} n_2 \tpi(n_2,n) y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad = \quad \lambda_1 \sum_{n_2=1}^{n-1} \tpi(n_2,n-1) y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad \quad + \lambda_2 \sum_{n_2=1}^{n-1} \tpi(n_2-1,n-1) y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad \quad + \mu \sum_{n_2=1}^{n-1} \tpi(n_2,n+1) y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad \quad + \gamma_1 \sum_{n_2=1}^{n-1} (n-n_2+1) \tpi(n_2-1,n)y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad \quad + \gamma_2 \sum_{n_2=1}^{n-1} (n_2+1) \tpi(n_2+1,n) y^{n_2}
    \end{split}
\end{align*}

We add our balance equation (Equation \eqref{eq:n1n_balance_diagonal}) multiplied by $y^n$ for the $n$-th term.

When adding these two equations, we notice that most terms from the diagonal equation are cleanly absorbed by the interior balance equation, extending the limit of the summation up to $n$. However, the $\gamma_1$-term on the left-hand side is extended to $n$ not by adding a term to the equation, but because the $(n-n_2)$ factor makes its $n$-th term evaluate to $0$. On the right-hand side, the coefficient $(n-n+1)=1$ exactly matches the $\gamma_1$ term added from the diagonal equation, allowing us to absorb it and complete the summation up to $n$ as well.         & + \gamma_2 \sum_{n_2=1}^{n} n_2 \tpi(n_2,n) y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad = \quad \lambda_1 \sum_{n_2=1}^{n-1} \tpi(n_2,n-1) y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad \quad + \lambda_2 \sum_{n_2=1}^{n} \tpi(n_2-1,n-1) y^{n_2} \\

Now we work the terms out individually:
\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
        & LHS_1 \\
        & \quad = \quad (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) \sum_{n_2=1}^{n} \tpi(n_2,n) y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad = \quad (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) \left[ \sum_{n_2=0}^{n} \tpi(n_2,n) y^{n_2} - \tpi(0,n) y^0 \right] \\
        & \quad = \quad (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) \left[ \widetilde{P}(y,n) - \tpi(0,n) \right]
    \end{split} \\
    \begin{split}
        & LHS_2 \\
        & \quad = \quad \gamma_1 \sum_{n_2=1}^{n} (n-n_2) \tpi(n_2,n)y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad = \quad \gamma_1 \left[ n \sum_{n_2=1}^{n} \tpi(n_2,n)y^{n_2} - \sum_{n_2=1}^{n} n_2 \tpi(n_2,n)y^{n_2} \right] \\
        & \quad = \quad \gamma_1 \left[ n \left( \sum_{n_2=0}^{n} \tpi(n_2,n) y^{n_2} - \tpi(0,n) y^0 \right) - y \sum_{n_2=1}^{n} n_2 \tpi(n_2,n) y^{n_2-1} \right] \\
        & \quad = \quad \gamma_1 \left[ n \big( \widetilde{P}(y,n) - \tpi(0,n) \big) - y \frac{d}{dy} \widetilde{P}(y,n) \right]
    \end{split} \\
    \begin{split}
        & LHS_3 \\
        & \quad = \quad \gamma_2 \sum_{n_2=1}^{n} n_2 \tpi(n_2,n) y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad = \quad \gamma_2 \left[ y \sum_{n_2=1}^{n} n_2 \tpi(n_2,n) y^{n_2-1} \right] \\
        & \quad = \quad \gamma_2 y \frac{d}{dy} \widetilde{P}(y,n)
    \end{split} \\
    \begin{split}
        & RHS_1 \\
        & \quad = \quad \lambda_1 \sum_{n_2=1}^{n-1} \tpi(n_2,n-1) y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad = \quad \lambda_1 \left[ \sum_{n_2=1}^{n} \tpi(n_2,n-1) y^{n_2} - \tpi(n,n-1) y^n \right] \\
        & \quad = \quad \lambda_1 \left[ \sum_{n_2=0}^{n} \tpi(n_2,n-1) y^{n_2} - \tpi(n,n-1) y^n - \tpi(0,n-1) y^0 \right] \\
        & \quad = \quad \lambda_1 \left[ \widetilde{P}(y,n-1) - \tpi(n,n-1)y^n - \tpi(0,n-1) \right]
    \end{split} \\
    \begin{split}
        & RHS_2 \\
        & \quad = \quad \lambda_2 \sum_{n_2=1}^{n} \tpi(n_2-1,n-1) y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad = \quad \lambda_2 \left[ y \sum_{n_2=1}^{n} \tpi(n_2-1,n-1) y^{n_2-1} \right] \\
        & \quad = \quad \lambda_2 \left[ y \sum_{n_2=0}^{n-1} \tpi(n_2,n-1) y^{n_2} \right] \\
        & \quad = \quad \lambda_2 \left[ y \left( \sum_{n_2=0}^{n} \tpi(n_2,n-1) y^{n_2} - \tpi(n,n-1)y^n \right) \right] \\
        & \quad = \quad \lambda_2 y \left[ \widetilde{P}(y,n-1) - \tpi(n,n-1)y^n \right]
    \end{split} \\
    \begin{split}
        & RHS_3 \\
        & \quad = \quad \mu \tpi(n+1,n+1) y^n + \mu \sum_{n_2=1}^{n} \tpi(n_2,n+1) y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad = \quad \mu \left[ \tpi(n+1,n+1) y^n + \sum_{n_2=1}^{n} \tpi(n_2,n+1) y^{n_2} \right] \\
        & \quad = \quad \mu \left[ \tpi(n+1,n+1) y^n + \sum_{n_2=0}^{n+1} \tpi(n_2,n+1) y^{n_2} - \tpi(0,n+1)y^0 - \tpi(n+1,n+1)y^{n+1} \right] \\

        & \quad = \quad \mu \left[ \widetilde{P}(y,n+1) - \tpi(0,n+1) + \tpi(n+1,n+1)y^n(1-y) \right]
    \end{split} \\
    \begin{split}
        & RHS_4 \\
        & \quad = \quad \gamma_1 \sum_{n_2=1}^{n} (n-n_2+1) \tpi(n_2-1,n)y^{n_2} \\

        & \quad = \quad \gamma_1 y \left[ n \sum_{k=0}^{n} \tpi(k,n) y^k - \sum_{k=0}^{n} k \tpi(k,n) y^k \right] \\
        & \quad = \quad \gamma_1 y \left[ n \widetilde{P}(y,n) - y \sum_{k=1}^{n} k \tpi(k,n) y^{k-1} \right] \\
        & \quad = \quad \gamma_1 y \left[ n \widetilde{P}(y,n) - y \frac{d}{dy} \widetilde{P}(y,n) \right]
    \end{split} \\
    \begin{split}
        & RHS_5 \\
        & \quad = \quad \gamma_2 \sum_{n_2=1}^{n-1} (n_2+1) \tpi(n_2+1,n) y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad = \quad \gamma_2 \sum_{k=2}^{n} k \tpi(k,n) y^{k-1} \quad \text{(letting $k = n_2+1$)} \\
        & \quad = \quad \gamma_2 \left[ \sum_{k=1}^{n} k \tpi(k,n) y^{k-1} - 1 \cdot \tpi(1,n) y^0 \right] \\
        & \quad = \quad \gamma_2 \left[ \frac{d}{dy} \widetilde{P}(y,n) - \tpi(1,n) \right]
    \end{split}
\end{align}

    \end{split}
\end{align*}
Grouping terms:
\begin{align*}

        & -(\gamma_2 + \gamma_1 y) (1-y) \frac{d}{dy} \widetilde{P}(y,n) + \left[ \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu + \gamma_1 n(1-y) \right] \widetilde{P}(y,n) \\
        & \quad = \quad (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 y) \widetilde{P}(y,n-1) + \mu \widetilde{P}(y,n+1) \\
        & \quad \quad + \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu + \gamma_1 n) \tpi(0,n) - \lambda_1 \tpi(0,n-1) - \mu \tpi(0,n+1) \right] - \gamma_2 \tpi(1,n) \\

Combine with $y$-boundary balance equation (Equation \eqref{eq:n1n_balance_y-boundary}):
\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
        & -(\gamma_2 + \gamma_1 y) (1-y) \frac{d}{dy} \widetilde{P}(y,n) + \left[ \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu + \gamma_1 n(1-y) \right] \widetilde{P}(y,n) \\
        & \quad = \quad (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 y) \widetilde{P}(y,n-1) + \mu \widetilde{P}(y,n+1) + \mu y^n (1-y) \tpi(n+1,n+1)
    \end{split} \label{eq:n1n_model-B_dynamics}
\end{align}

Sanity check 1, $\gamma_1=0, \gamma_2=0$:
\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
        & (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) \widetilde{P}(y,n) \\
        & \quad = \quad (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 y) \widetilde{P}(y,n-1) + \mu \widetilde{P}(y,n+1) + \mu y^n (1-y) \tpi(n+1,n+1)
    \end{split}
\end{align}

Sanity check 2, $y=1$:
\begin{align}
    (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) \widetilde{P}(1,n) \quad = \quad (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2) \widetilde{P}(1,n-1) + \mu \widetilde{P}(1,n+1)
\end{align}


\section{[NEW]Analysis model A: \texorpdfstring{$M/M/1$}{M/M/1} queue with no jockeying ($\gamma_1=0,\gamma_2=0$)}

The model discussed in this section is fundamentally more simple than the one explained in the previous sections. We set $\gamma_1=0,\gamma_2=0$, i.e. we do not allow our waiting customers to go to a different queue once they have arrived. The starting point for the analysis will depend on whether we decide to work with state space $S$ or with state space $\widetilde{S}$.

The balance equations are given —Equations \eqref{eq:n1n2_balance_interior}, \eqref{eq:n1n2_balance_x-boundary}, \eqref{eq:n1n2_balance_y-boundary}, \eqref{eq:n1n2_balance_zero} and \eqref{eq:n1n2_balance_zero} for state space $S$, and Equations \eqref{eq:n1n_balance_interior}, \eqref{eq:n1n_balance_diagonal}, \eqref{eq:n1n_balance_y-boundary}, \eqref{eq:n1n_balance_zero} and \eqref{eq:n1n_balance_idle} for state space $\widetilde{S}$ by plugging $\gamma_1=0$ and $\gamma_2=0$. The derivation follows accordingly, hence the starting point for the analysis of the model is the equation for the dynamics of our (P)PGF: this is Equation \eqref{eq:n1n2_model-b_fundamental_equation} for state space $S$ and Equation \eqref{eq:n1n_model-B_dynamics} for state space $\widetilde{S}$, for $\gamma_1=\gamma_2=0$. The corresponding subsections will introduce the "$\gamma$-less" versions of these equations.

\subsection{Analysis model A: state space $S$}

We start by stating a $\gamma$-free version of Equation \eqref{eq:n1n2_model-b_fundamental_equation}, where the function $P_y(y)$ and the constant $\pi(0,0)$ are still to be determined:
\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
        \left[ (\lambda_1+\lambda_2+ \mu)xy - \mu y - \lambda_1 x^2 y - \lambda_2 x y^2 \right] P(x,y) 
         &= \mu(x-y) P_y(y) 
         - \mu x(1-y) \pi(0,0).
    \end{split} \label{eq:n1n2_model-A_dynamics}
\end{align}

We now introduce a theorem, and the proofs for it —one analytical and one involving probabilistic arguments— will follow in the  subsections below.
\begin{theorem}
    Consider a queuing system with $2$ priority classes with Poisson arrival rates $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$, where class-$1$ customers have non-preemptive priority over those of class-$2$, and exponentially distributed service times with rate $\mu$ independently of the class.
 The probability generation function $P(x,y)$ of such a system is given by
    \begin{align}
        P(x,y) &= \left[ (1+\rho_1 +\rho_2)xy - y -\rho_1x^2y - \rho_2xy^2 \right]^{-1} \cdot y(1-y) \left[ \frac{x - x^*(y)}{x^*(y)-y} \right] \cdot \rho (1-\rho) \\[10pt]
        &\text{where} \nonumber \\[5pt]
        x^*(y) &= \frac{\left[\mu + \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 (1-y)\right] - \sqrt{\left[\mu + \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 (1-y)\right]^2 - 4 \lambda_1 \mu}}{2 \lambda_1}
    \end{align}
\end{theorem}

\subsubsection{Analytical approach of model A in state space $S$}

\begin{proof}
    We now address this question as a \textit{finite boundary problem}. Exploiting the fact that $P(x,y)$ is a PGF —and hence bounded— the statement below must follow:
    \begin{equation*}
        \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) xy - \mu y - \lambda_1 x^2 y - \lambda_2 x y^2 \right] P(x,y) = 0 
        \quad \Longrightarrow \quad
        \mu \big[ (x-y) P_y(y) - x(1-y) \pi(0,0) \big] = 0.
    \end{equation*}
    
    On the left-hand side of the statement, for $y\neq 0$, we have
    \begin{align}
        \begin{split}
             0 &=  (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu)x - \mu -\lambda_1x^2 - \lambda_2xy 
        \end{split} \nonumber \\
        \begin{split}
             0 &=  - \lambda_1 x^2 + \left[ \mu + \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 (1-y) \right]x - \mu 
        \end{split} \\
        \begin{split}
          0 &=  \lambda_1 x^2 - \left[ \mu + \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 (1-y) \right] x + \mu 
        \end{split} \nonumber \\
        \begin{split}
            x^*(y) &= \frac{
                \left[\mu + \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 (1-y) \right] \pm \sqrt{
                    \left[\mu + \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 (1-y) \right]^2 - 4  \mu \lambda_1
                }
            }{
                2\lambda_1
            }
        \end{split} \nonumber.
    \end{align}
    
    If we take $y=1$, we have
    \begin{align*}
        x^*(1) = \frac{(\mu + \lambda_1) + (\mu-\lambda_1)}{2 \lambda_1} = \frac{\mu}{\lambda_1} >1
        & \quad \text{and} \quad
        x^*(1) = \frac{(\mu + \lambda_1) - (\mu-\lambda_1)}{2 \lambda_1} = \frac{\lambda_1}{\lambda_1} =1.
    \end{align*}
    Since $x \in [0,1]$, only the option with the negative sign will be valid. Hence
    \begin{equation}
        x^*(y) = \frac{
            (\mu +\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 (1-y)) 
            - \sqrt{(\mu +\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 (1-y))^2 - 4 \lambda_1 \mu}
        }{
            2\lambda_1
        }.
        \label{eq:xstar}
    \end{equation}

    Since the next steps will involve using l'Hôpital's rule in order to compute a limit, another expression that we will need is the derivative of $x^*(y)$, i.e.  $\frac{d}{dy}x^*(y)$:
    \begin{align*}
        \frac{d}{dy}x^*(y)
        & = \frac{d}{dy} \left[ \frac{
            (\mu + \lambda_1 + \lambda_2(1-y))
            - \sqrt{(\mu + \lambda_1 + \lambda_2(1-y))^2 - 4\lambda_1\mu}
        }{2\lambda_1} \right] \\
        &= \frac{1}{2\lambda_1}
        \left[ -\lambda_2 - \frac{1}{2} \frac{2(\mu+\lambda_1+\lambda_2(1-y)) \cdot (-\lambda_2)}{
            \sqrt{(\mu + \lambda_1 + \lambda_2(1-y)) - 4\lambda_1 \mu}
        }\right] \\
        &= \frac{\lambda_2}{2 \lambda_1}
        \left[ 
            -1 + \frac{
                (\mu + \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 (1-y))
            }{
                \sqrt{(\mu+\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 (1-y))^2- 4\lambda_1\mu}
            }
        \right].
    \end{align*}
    More specifically, we need to see how this behaves when $y\to1$, so we compute the following:
    \begin{align*}
        \frac{d}{dy}x^*(1)
        &= \frac{\lambda_2}{2 \lambda_1}
        \left[ 
            -1 + \frac{
                \mu + \lambda_1
            }{
                \sqrt{(\mu+\lambda_1)^2- 4\lambda_1\mu}
            }
        \right]\\
        &= \frac{\lambda_2}{2\lambda_1}
        \left[ 
            -1 + \frac{
                \mu + \lambda_1
            }{
                \sqrt{(\mu-\lambda_1)^2}
            }
        \right]\\
        &= \frac{\lambda_2}{2\lambda_1}
        \left[
            \frac{\mu + \lambda_1 - (\mu - \lambda_1)}{\mu - \lambda_1}
        \right] \\
        &= \frac{\lambda_2}{\mu - \lambda_1} \\
        &= \frac{\rho_2}{1-\rho_1}
    \end{align*}
    For future reference,
    \begin{equation}
        \frac{d}{dy}x^*(1) = \frac{\rho_2}{1-\rho_1}.
    \end{equation}
    
    Now for $x=x^*(y)$, we have $\mu \big[ (x-y) P_y(y) - x(1-y) \pi(0,0) \big] = 0$, so
    \begin{equation}
        P_y(y) = \frac{x^*(y)(1-y)}{x^*(y) - y} \pi(0,0). \label{eq:pyy_xstars}
    \end{equation}
    Taking $(1-y)$ common factor gives us the following expression:
    \begin{equation}
         \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) xy - \mu y - \lambda_1 x^2 y - \lambda_2 x y^2 \right] P(x,y) = \mu (1-y) \left[ \frac{x^*(y) (x-y)}{x^*(y) - y} - x \right] \pi(0,0).
    \end{equation}
    Now, for x=1, the polynomial in the LHS of the expression above simplifies so that we can later divide both sides by $(1-y)$ and obtain the following.
    \begin{align*}
         \left[ \lambda_2 y(1-y) \right] P(1,y)
         &= \mu (1-y) \left[ \frac{x^*(y) (1-y)}{x^*(y) - y} - 1 \right] \pi(0,0) \\
         \lambda_2 y P(1,y)
         &= \mu \left[ \frac{x^*(y) (1-y)}{x^*(y) - y} - 1 \right] \pi(0,0) \\
         \rho_2 y P(1,y)
         &= \left[ \frac{x^*(y) (1-y)}{x^*(y) - y} - 1 \right] \pi(0,0) \\
    \end{align*}
    
    Since we know that $P(1,1)=\sum_{n_1=0}^{\infty}\sum_{n_2=0}^{\infty} \pi(n_1,n_2) = 1-\pi_0 = 1 - (1-\rho)= \rho$, we can take limits on both sides to obtain $\pi(0,0)$.
    
    \begin{equation}
        \lim_{y\to 1} \rho_2 y P(1,y) =  \lim_{y\to 1} \left[ \frac{x^*(y) (1-y)}{x^*(y) - y} - 1 \right] \pi(0,0) 
    \end{equation}
    
    Although the LHS of the above expression is simple, the RHS presents a type of indetermination "$0/0$". Because of that, we apply the L'Hôpital's rule and use the results previously obtained in Equations \eqref{eq:xstar} and \eqref{eq:xstar_1}:
    \begin{align*}
        \rho_2 \rho
        &=\left[
            \lim_{y\to1} \frac{\frac{d}{dy}(x^*(y)(1-y))}{\frac{d}{dy}(x^*(y)-y)}
            - 1
        \right] \pi(0,0) \\
        &= \lim_{y\to1} \left[
            \frac{
                \frac{d}{dy} x^*(y)(1-y) + x^*(y) (-1) 
            }{
                 \frac{d}{dy} x^*(y) - 1
            }
            -1
            \right]
        \pi(0,0)\\
        &= \left[ 
            \frac{
                \frac{d}{dy} x^*(1)(1-1) + x^*(1) (-1) 
            }{
                 \frac{d}{dy} x^*(1) - 1
            }
            - 1
            \right]
        \pi(0,0)\\
        &= \left[ 
            \frac{
                - 1
            }{
                \frac{\rho_2}{1-\rho_1} - 1
            }
            - 1
            \right]
        \pi(0,0)\\
        &= \left[ \frac{1}{1 - \frac{\rho_GJHTN2}{1-\rho_1}} -1 \right] \pi(0,0) \\
        &= \frac{1-\rho_1 - (1 - \rho_1 - \rho_2)}{1 - \rho_1 - \rho_2} \pi(0,0) \\
        &= \frac{\rho_2}{1 - \rho_1 - \rho_2} \pi(0,0)\\
        &= \frac{\rho_2}{1 - \rho} \pi(0,0) \\
    \end{align*}
    Thus, we found an expression for $\pi(0,0)$:
    \begin{equation}
        \pi(0,0) = \rho (1-\rho) \label{eq:pi00}
    \end{equation}

    Now that we have determined $P_y(y)$ and $\pi(0,0)$, substituting Equation \eqref{eq:pyy_xstars} and Equation \eqref{eq:pi00} into Equation \eqref{pxy_pyy-p00} yields
    \begin{align*}
        \left[ 
            (\lambda_1+\lambda_2+\mu)xy -\mu y-\lambda_1 x^2y - \lambda_2 xy^2
        \right] P(x,y)
        &= \mu \left[
            (x-y)\frac{x^*(y)(1-y)}{x^*(y)-y} - x(1-y)
        \right]
        \rho (1-\rho) \\
        \left[ 
            (\lambda_1+\lambda_2+\mu)xy -\mu y-\lambda_1 x^2y - \lambda_2 xy^2
        \right] P(x,y)
        &= \mu (1-y) \left[
            \frac{x^*(y)(x-y)}{x^*(y)-y} - x
        \right]
        \rho (1-\rho). \\
        \left[ 
            (\lambda_1+\lambda_2+\mu)xy -\mu y-\lambda_1 x^2y - \lambda_2 xy^2
        \right] P(x,y)
        &= \mu  (1-y) \left[
            \frac{y(x - x^*(y))}{x^*(y) - y}
        \right]
        \rho (1-\rho). \\
        \left[ 
            (1 + \rho_1 + \rho_2)xy - y-\rho_1 x^2y - \rho_2 xy^2
        \right] P(x,y)
        &= y (1-y) \left[
            \frac{x - x^*(y)}{x^*(y) - y}
        \right]
        \rho (1-\rho). \\
    \end{align*}
    
    Multiplying both sides by $[(\lambda_1+\lambda_2+\mu)xy -\mu y-\lambda_1 x^2y - \lambda_2 xy^2]^{-1}$ to isolate $P(x,y)$ concludes the proof.                                                                                          
\end{proof}




\subsubsection{Probabilistic approach of model A in state space $S$}
\begin{proof}
    Our goal here is to derive the partial PGF $P_y(y)$ based on probabilistic arguments. As done in the analytical approach, we start from the fundamental global balance equation for Model $A$, (i.e. Equation \eqref{eq:n1n2_model-b_fundamental_equation} with $\gamma_1=0, \gamma_2=0$):
    \begin{equation}
        \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) xy - \mu y - \lambda_1 x^2 y - \lambda_2 x y^2 \right] P(x,y) =
        \mu \big[ (x-y) P_y(y) - x(1-y) \pi(0,0) \big].
        \label{eq:global_balance_starting}
    \end{equation}
    
    Here, $P_y(y) = P(0,y) = \sum_{n_2=0}^{\infty} \pi(0,n_2)y^{n_2}$ represents the partial PGF restricted to states where the server is busy and the high-priority queue is empty ($N_1 = 0$). From the definition of conditional expectation, we can express $P_y(y)$ in the following way:
    \begin{equation}
        P_y(y) = \mathbb{P}(\text{busy}, N_1=0) \cdot \mathbb{E}[y^{N_2} \mid \text{busy}, N_1=0].
        \label{eq:pyy_prob_def}
    \end{equation}
    
    In order to address the term with the conditional expectation of class-$2$ customers, we must adopt their perspective. For non-priority customers, their queue behaves like an $M/G/1$-queue with arrival rate $\lambda_2$ and general service times $B$, where $B$ is distributed as the busy period of an $M/M/1$-queue with arrival rate $\lambda_1$ and exponentially distributed service times with rate $\mu$. 
    
    The LST for the busy period for the $M/M/1$-queue and its expectation are known results. We will directly refer to them as $\widetilde{B}(s)$ and $\mathbb{E}[B]$, where $B$ is already the effective service time of our $M/G/1$ subsystem for class-$2$:
    \begin{align}
        \widetilde{B}(s) &= \frac{\mu+\lambda_1+s - \sqrt{(\mu + \lambda_1 + s)^2-4\mu\lambda_1}}{2\lambda_1}, \\
        \mathbb{E}[B] &= \frac{1/\mu}{1-\rho_1}.
    \end{align}
    
 In this $M/G/1$ framework, we apply the Pollaczek-Khinchine formula to find $L_2(y)$, the unconditional generating function for the number of customers in this equivalent system. For an $M/G/1$ equivalent system with $\lambda_2$ arrivals and service times $B$ the Pollaczek-Khinchine formula reads:
    \begin{equation}
        L_2(y) = \frac{(1 - \lambda_2 \mathbb{E}[B]) (1-y) \widetilde{B}(\lambda_2(1-y))}{\widetilde{B}(\lambda_2(1-y)) - y}.
        \label{eq:n1n2_modelA_pk_formula}
    \end{equation}
    
    Notice that evaluating $\widetilde{B}(s)$ at $s = \lambda_2(1-y)$ yields the exact mathematical root $x^*(y)$ previously identified in Equation \eqref{eq:xstar}:
    \begin{equation}
        \widetilde{B}(\lambda_2(1-y)) = \frac{\mu+\lambda_1+\lambda_2(1-y) - \sqrt{(\mu + \lambda_1 + \lambda_2(1-y))^2-4\mu\lambda_1}}{2\lambda_1} = x^*(y).
    \end{equation}
    
    Substituting $\widetilde{B}(\lambda_2(1-y))=x^*(y)$ and $\lambda_2 \mathbb{E}[B] = \frac{\rho_2}{1-\rho_1}$ into the Pollaczek-Khinchine formula (Equation \eqref{eq:n1n2_modelA_pk_formula}) yields: 
    \begin{equation}
        L_2(y) =  \frac{1-\rho}{1-\rho_1} \cdot \frac{x^*(y)(1-y)}{x^*(y) - y}.
        \label{eq:l2_unconditional}
    \end{equation}
    
    Note that the service for class-$2$ customers is blocked during all states where $N_1>0$, which automatically implies the server is busy. Hence, the conditional distribution of the queue length given that $N_1=0$ and that the system is busy is equivalent to the unconditional distribution $L_2(y)$ of the equivalent $M/G/1$ system. Thus:
    \begin{equation}
        \mathbb{E}[y^{N_2} \mid \text{busy}, N_1=0] = L_2(y) = \frac{1-\rho}{1-\rho_1} \frac{x^*(y)(1-y)}{x^*(y) - y}.
        \label{eq:conditional_n2}
    \end{equation}
    
    Returning to the probabilistic definition of $P_y(y)$, we can now use this $L_2(y)$ together with the fact that $\mathbb{P}(\text{busy},N_1=0)=\rho(1-\rho_1)$:
    \begin{align*}
         &P_y(y) \\
         & \quad=\quad \mathbb{P}(\text{busy}, N_1=0) \cdot \mathbb{E}[y^{N_2} \mid \text{busy}, N_1=0] \\
         & \quad=\quad \rho(1-\rho_1) \cdot \left( \frac{1-\rho}{1-\rho_1} \cdot \frac{x^*(y)(1-y)}{x^*(y) - y} \right)\\
         &\quad=\quad \rho (1-\rho) \cdot \frac{x^*(y)(1-y)}{x^*(y) - y}. 
    \end{align*}
    
    We now substitute $P_y(y)$ and $\pi(0,0)=\rho(1-\rho)$ back into the right-hand side of our starting global balance Equation \eqref{eq:global_balance_starting}:
    \begin{align*}
        \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) xy - \mu y - \lambda_1 x^2 y - \lambda_2 x y^2 \right] P(x,y) 
        &= \mu \left[ (x-y) \left( \rho(1-\rho) \frac{x^*(y)(1-y)}{x^*(y) - y} \right) - x(1-y) \rho(1-\rho) \right].
    \end{align*}
    
    Factoring out the common terms $\mu \rho (1-\rho) (1-y)$ gives:
    \begin{align*}
        &\left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) xy - \mu y - \lambda_1 x^2 y - \lambda_2 x y^2 \right] P(x,y) \\
        &\quad=\quad \mu (1-y) \left[ \frac{(x-y)x^*(y)}{x^*(y) - y} - x \right] \rho(1-\rho)\\
        &\quad=\quad  \mu (1-y) \left[ \frac{x \cdot x^*(y) - y \cdot x^*(y) - x(x^*(y) - y)}{x^*(y) - y} \right] \rho(1-\rho)\\
        &\quad=\quad  \mu (1-y) \left[ \frac{xy - y \cdot x^*(y)}{x^*(y) - y} \right]\rho(1-\rho) \\
        &\quad=\quad  \mu y(1-y) \left[ \frac{x - x^*(y)}{x^*(y) - y} \right]\rho(1-\rho).
    \end{align*}
    
    Dividing both sides by $\mu$ yields the following:
    \begin{equation*}
        \left[ (1 + \rho_1 + \rho_2) xy - y - \rho_1 x^2 y - \rho_2 x y^2 \right] P(x,y) = y(1-y) \left[ \frac{x - x^*(y)}{x^*(y) - y} \right]\rho(1-\rho).
    \end{equation*}
    
    And, finally, multiplying both sides by $\left[ (1+\rho_1 +\rho_2)xy - y -\rho_1x^2y - \rho_2xy^2 \right]^{-1}$ completes the proof:
    \begin{equation}
        P(x,y) = \left[ (1+\rho_1 +\rho_2)xy - y -\rho_1x^2y - \rho_2xy^2 \right]^{-1}  y(1-y) \left[ \frac{x - x^*(y)}{x^*(y)-y} \right] \rho (1-\rho).
    \end{equation}
\end{proof}




\subsubsection{PPGF approach of model A in state space $S$}

In this subsection, we will approach the balance equations by keeping the first variable $N_1$ intact and taking the PGF only with respect to the second variable $N_2$. By multiplying our equations by $y^{n_2}$ and adding them all across their respective domains, we will find some new relations that describe the dynamics of the class-$2$ queue. The idea here is that, given that queue-$1$ is a regular $M/M/1$ queue with Poisson arrivals at rate $\lambda_1$ and service rate $\mu$, we can consider it to be fairly easy to describe.

From the interior balance equation, then, we obtain
\begin{align*}
    \begin{split}
       & (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} \pi(n_1,n_2) y^{n_2} \\
        &\quad = \quad\mu \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} \pi(n_1+1,n_2) y^{n_2}
        + \lambda_1 \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} \pi(n_1-1,n_2) y^{n_2} 
        + \lambda_2 \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} \pi(n_1,n_2-1) y^{n_2}
    \end{split} \\
    \begin{split}
        & (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) \left[ \sum_{n_2=0}^{\infty} \pi(n_1,n_2) y^{n_2} - \pi(n_1,0) \right] \\
        & \quad = \quad\mu \left[ \sum_{n_2=0}^{\infty} \pi(n_1+1,n_2) y^{n_2} - \pi(n_1+1,0) \right]\\
        &\quad\quad+ \lambda_1 \left[ \sum_{n_2=0}^{\infty} \pi(n_1-1,n_2) y^{n_2} - \pi(n_1-1,0) \right]\\
        &\quad\quad+ \lambda_2 y \sum_{n_2=0}^{\infty} \pi(n_1,n_2) y^{n_2} 
    \end{split} \\
    \begin{split}
        &(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) \left[ \widetilde{P}(n_1,y) - \pi(n_1,0) \right] \\
        & \quad= \quad \mu \left[ \widetilde{P}(n_1+1, y) - \pi(n_1+1,0)\right]
        + \lambda_1 \left[ \widetilde{P}(n_1-1,y) - \pi(n_1-1,0) \right]
        + \lambda_2 y \widetilde{P}(n_1,y)
    \end{split} 
\end{align*}

Note that we can use the $x$-boundary equation (Equation \eqref{eq:balance2}) to simplify the expression. Doing so and then grouping terms yields
\begin{equation}
    \left[ \mu + \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 (1-y) \right] \widetilde{P}(n_1,y)
    = \mu \widetilde{P}(n_1+1,y)
    + \lambda_1 \widetilde{P}(n_1-1,y).
    \label{eq:model-i_ppgf_recurrence}
\end{equation}

Cohen notices that since $\widetilde{P}(n_1,y)$ is only defined in terms of its neighbouring states, there exists a recurrent relation such that
\begin{equation}
    \widetilde{P}(n_1, y) = \widetilde{P}(0,y) [\widetilde{x}^*(y)]^{n_1},
\end{equation}
where $\widetilde{x}^*(y)$ is a root of the characteristic equation
\begin{equation}
    \mu [\widetilde{x}^*(y)]^2 - [\mu + \lambda_1 + \lambda_2(1-y)] \widetilde{x}^*(y) + \lambda_1 = 0.
\end{equation}

A similar procedure can be replicated with the $y$-boundary balance equation (Equation \eqref{eq:balance3}) to obtain $\widetilde{P}(0,y)$. First, solving the characteristic equation yields two roots:
\begin{equation*}
    \widetilde{x}^*(y) = \frac{(\mu + \lambda_1 + \lambda_2(1-y)) \pm \sqrt{(\mu + \lambda_1 + \lambda_2(1-y))^2 - 4\lambda_1\mu}}{2\mu}.
\end{equation*}

Cohen selects the root with the negative sign because $\widetilde{P}(n_1, y)$ is a (P)PGF and thus must be analytic and bounded. Because we want the geometric series $\sum_{n_1=0}^{\infty} \widetilde{P}(0, y) [\widetilde{x}^*(y)]^{n_1}$ to converge, our root must satisfy $|\widetilde{x}^*(y)| \leq 1$. When evaluating both roots at $y=1$, the root with the negative sign evaluates to $\rho_1$, whereas the root with the positive sign evaluates to $1$. Only the root with the negative sign ensures the convergence of our geometric series, hence:
\begin{equation}
    \widetilde{x}^*(y) = \frac{(\mu + \lambda_1 + \lambda_2(1-y)) - \sqrt{(\mu + \lambda_1 + \lambda_2(1-y))^2 - 4\lambda_1\mu}}{2\mu}.
\end{equation}

By evaluating the roots at the marginal case $y=1$, the characteristic polynomial simplifies to $(\mu \widetilde{x}^*(1) - \lambda_1)(\widetilde{x}^*(1) - 1) = 0$, resulting in roots $\widetilde{x}^*(1) = \rho_1$ and $\widetilde{x}^*(1) = 1$. In a stable system where $\rho_1 < 1$, the root with the positive sign would lead to a value greater than $1$ for certain values of $y$, which implies a divergent queue with infinite expected length. Only the root with the negative sign remains within the unit circle for all $|y| \leq 1$, ensuring the stability of the priority class.

Note that at $y=1$, this result simplifies to the marginal priority traffic intensity:
\begin{equation}
    \widetilde{x}^*(1) = \frac{\lambda_1}{\mu} = \rho_1.
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Analytical approach of model A in state space $\widetilde{S}$, neglecting the non-homogeneous term}

For state space $\widetilde{S}$, the dynamics for the PPGF are described by the following inhomogeneous difference equation:
\begin{equation}
    (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) \widetilde{P}(y,n) = (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 y) \widetilde{P}(y,n-1) + \mu \widetilde{P}(y,n+1) + \mu y^n (1-y) \pi(n+1,n+1).
\end{equation}
The fact that it's not homogeneous implies that we are not allowed to use Cohen's trick where $\tP(y,n)=\tP(y,0)\cdot [f(y)]^n$. As a consequence, it becomes rather challenging to solve. But, given than the inhomogeneous term $\mu y^n (1-y) \pi(n+1,n+1)$ shows exponential decay, we can try to obtain an approximation by neglecting it. In that case we are left with
\begin{equation}
    (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) \widetilde{P}(y,n) = (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 y) \widetilde{P}(y,n-1) + \mu \widetilde{P}(y,n+1).
\end{equation}
By equating it to $0$, we can obtain the characteristic polynomial of this difference equation:
\begin{equation}
    \mu [\widetilde{y}(y)]^2 - (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) \widetilde{y}(y) + (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 y) = 0,
\end{equation}
where solving for $\widetilde{y}(y)$ yields
\begin{equation}
    \widetilde{y}^*(y) = \frac{(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) \pm \sqrt{(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu)^2 - 4\mu(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 y)}}{2\mu}.
\end{equation}
Plugging in $y=1$ already lets us exclude the positive root —$\widetilde{y}_{+}(y)=1$ and $\widetilde{y}_{-}(y)=\rho$— and leaves us with
\begin{equation}
    \widetilde{y}^*(y) = \frac{(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) - \sqrt{(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu)^2 - 4\mu(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 y)}}{2\mu}.
\end{equation}
Applying Cohen's trick yields
\begin{equation}
    \tP(y,n)=\tP(y,0) \cdot  [\widetilde{y}^*(y)]^n.
\end{equation}
Using $\tP(y,0)=\sum_{n_2=0}^0\tpi(n_2,0)y^{n_2}=\tpi(0,0)=(1-\rho)\rho$, we obtain
\begin{equation}
    \tP(y,n)=(1-\rho)\rho \cdot  [\widetilde{y}^*(y)]^n.
\end{equation}
We can evaluate this expression at $y=1$ and check that it will behave like a regular $M/M/1$ queueing system with arrival rate $\lambda=\lambda_1+\lambda_2$ and service rate $\mu$. Note that, since $n$ counts the total number of customers in the queues and not in the system, we will have an exponent $n+1$ in the $\rho$ term, with thar $+1$ accounting for the customer in service:
\begin{equation}
    \tP(1,n)= (1-\rho)\rho \cdot  [\widetilde{y}^*(1)]^n = (1-\rho)\rho \cdot\rho^n= (1-\rho) \rho^{n+1}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Analysis model B by the Method of Characteristics and the Method of Variation of Parameters}

To solve the homogeneous part of the PDE for this model (Equation \eqref{eq:n1n2_model-b_fundamental_equation}), we set the right-hand side to zero and proceed with the Method of Characteristics.

We start by stating the homogeneous version of the equation:
\begin{equation*}
    \left[ (\lambda_1+\lambda_2+ \mu)xy - \mu y - \lambda_1 x^2 y - \lambda_2 x y^2 \right] P(x,y) + xy(x-y) \left[ \gamma_1 \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} - \gamma_2 \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \right] = 0
\end{equation*}
We take common factor $y$ and divide by it. Thus, for $y \neq 0$ we have:
\begin{equation*}
    \left[ (\lambda_1+\lambda_2+ \mu)x - \mu - \lambda_1 x^2 - \lambda_2 xy \right] P(x,y) + x(x-y) \left[ \gamma_1 \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} - \gamma_2 \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \right] = 0
\end{equation*}
Rearranging this into the standard form of a linear first-order PDE yields the following expression:
\begin{equation*}
    \gamma_1 x(x-y) \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} - \gamma_2 x(x-y) \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = - \left[ (\lambda_1+\lambda_2+ \mu)x - \mu - \lambda_1 x^2 - \lambda_2 xy \right] P
\end{equation*}

Applying the Method of Characteristics, we construct the following system of ODEs:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{dx}{\gamma_1 x(x-y)} = \frac{dy}{-\gamma_2 x(x-y)} = \frac{dP}{- \left[ (\lambda_1+\lambda_2+ \mu)x - \mu - \lambda_1 x^2 - \lambda_2 xy \right] P}
\end{equation*}
We take the first pair of equations to find the relationship between $x$ and $y$:
\begin{equation*}
    \frac{dx}{\gamma_1} = \frac{dy}{-\gamma_2}
\end{equation*}
Integrating this yields our characteristic constant $C_1$:
\begin{equation}
    -\gamma_2 x = \gamma_1 y - C_1 \implies C_1 = \gamma_2 x + \gamma_1 y
    \label{eq:n1n2_modelB_pde_characteristic_constant}
\end{equation}
Now we can parameterise $y$ in terms of $x$ along the characteristic curves: 
\begin{equation*}
    y = \frac{C_1 - \gamma_2 x}{\gamma_1}.
\end{equation*}
Next, we use the $dx$ and $dP$ relation, substituting the parametric expression for $y$:
\begin{align*}
    \begin{split}
        \frac{dP}{P} &= \frac{- \left[ (\lambda_1+\lambda_2+\mu)x - \mu - \lambda_1 x^2 - \lambda_2 x \left(\frac{C_1 - \gamma_2 x}{\gamma_1}\right) \right]}{\gamma_1 x \left( x - \frac{C_1 - \gamma_2 x}{\gamma_1} \right)} dx
    \end{split} \\
    \begin{split}
        \frac{dP}{P} &= \frac{- \left[ \gamma_1(\lambda_1+\lambda_2+\mu)x - \gamma_1\mu - \gamma_1\lambda_1 x^2 - \lambda_2 C_1 x + \lambda_2 \gamma_2 x^2 \right]}{\gamma_1 x \left( (\gamma_1+\gamma_2)x - C_1 \right)} dx.
    \end{split} \\
    \begin{split}
        \frac{dP}{P} &= \frac{(\gamma_1\lambda_1 - \lambda_2\gamma_2)x^2 - \left[ \gamma_1(\lambda_1+\lambda_2+\mu) - \lambda_2 C_1 \right]x + \gamma_1\mu}{\gamma_1 x \left( (\gamma_1+\gamma_2)x - C_1 \right)} dx
    \end{split}
\end{align*}

We now proceed to apply Partial Fraction Decomposition. Let the integrand be $\frac{1}{\gamma_1} I(x) dx$, where:
\begin{equation*}
    I(x) = K + \frac{M}{x} + \frac{N}{(\gamma_1+\gamma_2)x - C_1}
\end{equation*}
By matching coefficients, we find the constants $K$, $M$, and $N$:
\begin{align*}
    K = \frac{\gamma_1\lambda_1 - \lambda_2\gamma_2}{\gamma_1+\gamma_2}, \quad M = -\frac{\gamma_1\mu}{C_1}, \quad \text{and} \quad N(C_1) = \gamma_1 \left[ \frac{\lambda_1+\lambda_2}{\gamma_1+\gamma_2} C_1 - (\lambda_1+\lambda_2+\mu) + \frac{\mu(\gamma_1+\gamma_2)}{C_1} \right].
\end{align*}
We therefore have the following.
\begin{align*}
    \begin{split}
        \frac{dP}{P} &= \frac{1}{\gamma_1}\left( K - \frac{\gamma_1\mu}{C_1 x} + \frac{N(C_1)}{(\gamma_1+\gamma_2)x - C_1} \right) dx
    \end{split} \\
    \begin{split}
        \int \frac{dP}{P} &= \frac{1}{\gamma_1} \int \left( K - \frac{\gamma_1\mu}{C_1 x} + \frac{N(C_1)}{(\gamma_1+\gamma_2)x - C_1} \right) dx
    \end{split} \\
    \begin{split}
        \ln P &= \frac{K}{\gamma_1} x - \frac{\mu}{C_1} \ln x 
    + \frac{N(C_1)}{\gamma_1(\gamma_1+\gamma_2)} \ln\left( (\gamma_1+\gamma_2)x - C_1 \right) + \ln f(C_1)
    \end{split},
\end{align*}
where $f(C_1)$ is an arbitrary function of the characteristic constant.

Note that $(\gamma_1+\gamma_2)x - C_1 = (\gamma_1+\gamma_2)x - (\gamma_2 x + \gamma_1 y) = \gamma_1(x-y)$. The extra $\ln(\gamma_1)$ constant can be absorbed into the arbitrary function $f(C_1)$ to form a new function $F(C_1)$. We now substitute $C_1 = \gamma_2 x + \gamma_1 y$ into the equation. Taking the exponential on both sides yields the general solution to the homogeneous PDE:
\begin{equation}
    P_h(x,y) = F(\gamma_2 x + \gamma_1 y) \cdot \exp\left( \frac{\gamma_1\lambda_1 - \lambda_2\gamma_2}{\gamma_1(\gamma_1+\gamma_2)} x \right) \cdot x^{-\frac{\mu}{\gamma_2 x + \gamma_1 y}} \cdot (x-y)^{E(x,y)},
\end{equation}
where $F$ is an arbitrary differentiable function to be determined by our boundary conditions, and  $E(x,y)$ is given by:
\begin{equation}
    E(x,y) = \frac{\lambda_1+\lambda_2}{(\gamma_1+\gamma_2)^2} (\gamma_2 x + \gamma_1 y) - \frac{\lambda_1+\lambda_2+\mu}{\gamma_1+\gamma_2} + \frac{\mu}{\gamma_2 x + \gamma_1 y}.
\end{equation}

The immediate steps after deriving the homogeneous solution by the Method of Characteristics are called the Method of Variation of Parameters. The idea here is to parameterize $y$ in terms of $x$ such that the pair $(x,y)$ lies in our characteristic curve. For $C_1=\gamma_2x+\gamma_1y$, we have 
\begin{equation}
    y(x) = \frac{C_1-\gamma_2x}{\gamma_1}.
\end{equation}

Now the problem reduces to the following first-order ODE in $x$:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{dP}{dx} =  \underbrace{-\frac{A(x, y(x))}{\gamma_1 x (x - y(x))}}_{f(x;C_1)} P + \underbrace{\frac{G(x, y(x))}{\gamma_1 x y(x) (x - y(x))}}_{h(x;C_1)}
    = f(x;C_1) P + h(x;C_1)
\end{equation}
where
\begin{align}
    A(x,y) &= (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu)x - \mu - \lambda_1 x^2 - \lambda_2xy,  \\
    G(x,y) &= \mu (x-y) P_y(y) -\mu x(1-y)\pi(0,0).
\end{align}

Since $P_h(x,y(x))$, for a fixed $C_1$, is a solution to the homogeneous problem $\frac{dP_h}{dx}= f(x;C_1)P_h$ —hence $e^{\int fdx}=P_h$, up to a multiplicative constant—, it is valid as a fundamental solution for the Method of Variation of Parameters. Therefore, using $P_h^{-1}(x,y(x))$ as the integrating factor for this ODE, the complete solution along the characteristic curve reads
\begin{equation}
    P(x,y(x)) = P_h(x,y(x))\left[F(C_1) + \int_{x_0}^{x} \frac{h(t;C_1)}{P_h(t,y(t))} \,dt\right]
\end{equation}

Note that when restricted to a characteristic curve $y(t)$, our forcing function $h(t;C_1)$ simplifies dramatically. By directly applying $G(t, y(t))$ into the numerator, we get:
\begin{align}
    h(t;C_1) &= \frac{\mu(t - y(t)) P_y(y(t)) - \mu t(1-y(t))\pi(0,0)}{\gamma_1 t y(t) (t - y(t))} \nonumber \\
    &= \frac{\mu(t - y(t)) P_y(y(t))}{\gamma_1 t y(t) (t - y(t))} - \frac{\mu t(1-y(t))\pi(0,0)}{\gamma_1 t y(t) (t - y(t))} \nonumber \\
    &= \frac{\mu}{\gamma_1 t y(t)} P_y(y(t)) - \frac{\mu(1 - y(t))}{\gamma_1 y(t)(t - y(t))} \pi(0, 0).
\end{align}

Notice $P_h(x,y(x))$ contains a term $x^{-\frac{\mu}{C_1}}$. Since $\mu \geq 0$ and $C_1=\gamma_2x+\gamma_1y \geq 0$, that term would blow up to infinity for $x\to0$, while all other terms in $P_h(x,y(x))$ remain finite at $x=0$.
Usually $x_0$ is one of the problematic values, such as $x_0=0$. For $x\to0$, we have
\begin{equation}
    \lim_{x \to 0} P(x,y(x)) = \lim_{x \to 0} \left( P_h(x,y(x))\left[F(C_1) + \int_{0}^{x} \frac{h(t;C_1)}{P_h(t,y(t))} \,dt\right] \right).
\end{equation}
The value of the integral above is exactly $0$, since the limits are the same. Since $P_h$ explodes to infinity, we are left with the following for $x\to x_0$
\begin{equation}
    P(0,y(0)) \to \infty \cdot [ F(C_1)+0 ] \quad \quad\text{as} \quad\quad x \to 0^+.
\end{equation}
This forces $F(C_1)=0$, since any different value would make our PGF diverge. Therefore, we now have the following:
\begin{equation}
    P(x,y(x)) = P_h(x,y(x))\int_{0}^{x} \frac{h(t;C_1)}{P_h(t,y(t))} \,dt.
\end{equation}

Now we have defined $P()x,y(x)$ in terms of the homogeneous solution $P_h(x,y(x))$ and an integral containing that same homogeneous solution and our forcing function $h(t;C_1)$. But the forcing function still contains an unknown $y$-boundary function $P_y(y)=\sum_{n_2=0}^{\infty} \pi(0,n_2)y^{n_2}$ —in fact, $P_y(y(t))$, since it is inside an integral—. In order to solve for our boundary function, we can exploit our diagonal singularity $x=y$, where we look at the total number of customers in the queues. To avoid confusion, we will write $z=x=y$.

Every point $(x,y)$ lies on exactly one characteristic curve given by our characteristic constant $C_1=\gamma_2x+\gamma_1y$. For a point $(z,z)$ in the diagonal, we have out characteristic constant $C_1 = (\gamma_1+\gamma_2)z$. Along that curve, we will have the following parametrization:
\begin{equation}
    y(t\,;\,z) = \frac{(\gamma_1+\gamma_2)z - \gamma_2\,t}{\gamma_1}.
\end{equation}
Notice that $y(0\,;\,z)> z$ an that $y(z\,;\,z)= z$ —which confirms that the curve hits the diagonal. For every other point in between, i.e. in the interval $t \in [0,z)$ we will have $y(t\,;\,z) > t$, which we will now use.

The forcing function $h(t;C_1)$ in the Method of Variation of Parameters, along the characteristic curve where $C_1=(\gamma_1+\gamma_2)z$, becomes
\begin{equation}
    h(t\,;\,C_1) = \frac{\mu}{\gamma_1\,t\,y(t;z)}\,P_y\!\bigl(y(t;z)\bigr)- \frac{\mu\,\bigl(1-y(t;z)\bigr)}{\gamma_1\,y(t;z)\,\bigl(t-y(t;z)\bigr)}\,\pi(0,0).
\end{equation}
Now we use the fact that $y(t\,;\,z) > t$ for $t \in [0,z)$ to change the sign in the equation above, as the denominator $(t - y(t;z))$ is negative. We obtain
\begin{equation}
    h(t\,;\,C_1) = \frac{\mu}{\gamma_1\,t\,y(t;z)}\,P_y\!\bigl(y(t;z)\bigr) + \frac{\mu\,\bigl(1-y(t;z)\bigr)}{\gamma_1\,y(t;z)\,\bigl(y(t;z)-t\bigr)}\,\pi(0,0).
\end{equation}

From the derivation in Model A for the total number of customers, we can use the result $P(z,z)=\frac{\pi(0,0)}{1-\rho z}$. This result also holds for Model B, as the jockeying containing $\gamma_1$ or $\gamma_2$ terms appear multiplied by $(x-y)$ in our fundamental equation (Equation \eqref{eq:n1n2_model-b_fundamental_equation}). Intuitively, jockeying does not alter the total number of customers. Next, we evaluate the integral formula at $x=z$. Note the upper limit of the integral also hits the diagonal point $z$. We obtain
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\pi(0,0)}{1-\rho z} = P_h(z,z)\int_0^z \frac{h\bigl(t\,;\,(\gamma_1+\gamma_2)z\bigr)}{P_h\bigl(t,\,y(t;z)\bigr)}\,dt.
\end{equation}

From the step where we rearranged the forcing function, substitute $h$ and distribute $P_h(z,z)$, as only the part containing $P_y(y(t;z))$ is unknown whereas the other part depends only on fully known constants and functions:
\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
        &\frac{\pi(0,0)}{1-\rho z} \\
        &\quad = \quad \frac{\mu\,P_h(z,z)}{\gamma_1}  \int_0^z \frac{P_y\bigl(y(t;z)\bigr)}{t\cdot y(t;z)\cdot P_h\bigl(t,y(t;z)\bigr)}\,dt \\
        & \quad \quad + \frac{\mu\,P_h(z,z)}{\gamma_1}   \int_0^z \frac{\bigl(1-y(t;z)\bigr)\,\pi(0,0)}{y(t;z)\cdot\bigl(y(t;z)-t\bigr)\cdot P_h\bigl(t,y(t;z)\bigr)}\,dt.
    \end{split}
\end{align}

To put the problem in a rather more standard form, we will define the known and the unknown part. Let
\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
        \mathcal{K}(z,t) := \frac{\mu\cdot P_h(z,z)} {\gamma_1\cdot t\cdot y(t;z)\cdot P_h\bigl(t,y(t;z)\bigr)},
    \end{split} \\
    \begin{split}
        \mathcal{B}(z) := \frac{\mu\cdot P_h(z,z)\cdot\pi(0,0)}{\gamma_1} \int_0^z \frac{1-y(t;z)}{y(t;z)\cdot\bigl(y(t;z)-t\bigr)\cdot P_h\bigl(t,y(t;z)\bigr)}\,dt.
    \end{split}
\end{align}
Having defined those, we have the following expression:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\pi(0,0)}{1-\rho z} = \int_0^z \mathcal{K}(z,t)\,P_y\!\bigl(y(t;z)\bigr)\,dt + \mathcal{B}(z).
\end{equation}
Persisting on this task of grouping known and unknown terms, we define $\mathcal{F}(z)=\frac{\pi(0,0)}{1-\rho z}-\mathcal{B}(z)$. Therefore
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{F}(z) = \int_0^z \mathcal{K}(z,t)\,P_y\!\bigl(y(t;z)\bigr)\,dt.
\end{equation}
This is an almost standard form of a type of equations called \textit{Volterra equation of the first kind} for the $y$-boundary function $P_y$. By carrying out a change of variables $s=y(t;z)$ and the subsequent steps would leave the equation in the standard form for equations of this kind. However, these steps are sufficient for us to determine that, if the derivation that brought us to this point was already quite demanding, solving such an equation is far beyond the scope of this thesis, as very advanced mathematical methods are needed here.

\subsubsection{Variant Model $B_2$ ($\gamma_2=0$)}

Let $\gamma_2=0$, then our fundamental equation states:
\begin{equation}
    \bigl[(\lambda_1+\lambda_2+\mu)xy-\mu y-\lambda_1 x^2 y-\lambda_2 xy^2\bigr]P + \gamma_1 xy(x-y)\frac{\partial P}{\partial x} = \mu(x-y)P_y(y) - \mu x(1-y)\pi(0,0).
\end{equation}
Due to the absence of a $\frac{\partial P}{\partial y}$ term, for any fixed $y$, we will have a first-order linear ODE in $x$. Now, for $y\neq0$, we have
\begin{equation}
    \bigl[(\lambda_1+\lambda_2+\mu)x-\mu -\lambda_1 x^2 -\lambda_2 xy\bigr]P + \gamma_1 x(x-y)\frac{\partial P}{\partial x} = \frac{\mu(x-y)P_y(y)}{y} - \frac{\mu x(1-y)\pi(0,0)}{y}.
\end{equation}
Next, we leave the ODE in standard form by dividing both sides by $\gamma_1 x(x-y)$:
\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
        \frac{dP}{dx} + \underbrace{\frac{(\lambda_1+\lambda_2+\mu)x - \mu - \lambda_1 x^2 - \lambda_2 xy}{\gamma_1 x(x-y)}}_{p(x;\,y)}\,P &= \underbrace{\frac{\mu\,P_y(y)}{\gamma_1 xy} - \frac{\mu(1-y)\pi(0,0)}{\gamma_1 y(x-y)}}_{q(x;\,y)} 
    \end{split} \\
    \begin{split}
        \frac{dP}{dx} + p(x;y) P &= q(x;y)
    \end{split}
\end{align}
Here we can write the numerator of $p(x;y)$ as we already know its roots —we write $x^*(y)$ for the taking the negative sign (Equation \eqref{eq:xstar}) and $x_+(y)$ for the root with the positive sign. Hence, we write $(\lambda_1+\lambda_2+\mu)x - \mu - \lambda_1 x^2 - \lambda_2 xy = -\lambda_1 (x-x^*(y))(x-x^+(y))$.
\begin{equation}
    \frac{dP}{dx} - \frac{\lambda_1 (x-x^*(y))(x-x^+(y))}{\gamma_1 x(x-y)}P = q(x;y).
\end{equation}
We can carry on Partial Fraction Decomposition on $p(x;y)$:
\begin{align}
    - \frac{\lambda_1 (x-x^*(y))(x-x^+(y))}{ x(x-y)} = A + \frac{B}{x} +\frac{C}{x-y},
\end{align}
where $A=-\lambda_1$ is obtained by taking the leading coefficient of the $x^2$-term, $B=\frac{\mu}{y}$ by setting $x=0$, and $C=\frac{(1-y)((\lambda_1+\lambda_2)y-\mu)}{y}$ is obtained by setting $x=y$. So
\begin{equation}
    \frac{-\lambda_1(x-x^*)(x-x_+)}{x(x-y)} = -\lambda_1 + \frac{\mu/y}{x} + \frac{(1-y)(\lambda y-\mu)/y}{x-y}.
\end{equation}
If we integrate term by term we obtain
\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
        \int p(x;\,y)\,dx &= \frac{1}{\gamma_1}\!\left[-\lambda_1 x + \frac{\mu}{y}\ln x + \frac{(1-y)(\lambda y-\mu)}{y}\ln(x-y)\right]
    \end{split} \\
    \begin{split}
        \mu_I(x;\,y) &= e^{-\lambda_1 x/\gamma_1}\cdot x^{\,\alpha(y)}\cdot(x-y)^{\beta(y)},
    \end{split}
\end{align}
where
\begin{align}
    \alpha(y)&=\frac{\mu}{\gamma_1 y} \\
    \beta(y)&=\frac{(1-y)(\lambda y-\mu)}{\gamma_1 y}.
\end{align}
Integrating the standard identity $\frac{d}{dx}P\cdot \mu_I= q \cdot \mu_I$ on the interval $(0,x]$, we get
\begin{equation}
    P(x,y)\,\mu_I(x;\,y) - P(0,y)\,\mu_I(0;\,y) = \int_0^x q(t;\,y)\,\mu_I(t;\,y)\,dt.
\end{equation}
Note $\mu_I(x;t) \to 0 \quad$ as $x\to0^+$, so the boundary term vanishes:
\begin{equation}
    P(x,y)\,\mu_I(x;\,y)
= \int_0^x q(t;\,y)\,\mu_I(t;\,y)\,dt.
\end{equation}
Now we substitute $q(x;t)=\frac{\mu\,P_y(y)}{\gamma_1 xy}
  - \frac{\mu(1-y)\pi(0,0)}{\gamma_1 y(x-y)}$ and divide by $\mu(x;y)$. Note $P_y(y)$ acts as a constant inside our integral, since it only contains terms on $y$. We can define the integrals:
\begin{align}
    \mathcal{I}_1(x,y) &= \int_0^x e^{-\lambda_1 t/\gamma_1}\,t^{\,\alpha-1}(t-y)^{\beta}\,dt, \\
    \mathcal{I}_2(x,y) &= \int_0^x e^{-\lambda_1 t/\gamma_1}\,t^{\,\alpha}(t-y)^{\beta-1}\,dt
\end{align}
So we obtain
\begin{equation}
    P(x,y) =
\frac{e^{\lambda_1 x/\gamma_1}\,x^{-\alpha(y)}\,(x-y)^{-\beta(y)}}{\gamma_1 y}
\Bigl[\,\mu\,P_y(y)\,\mathcal{I}_1(x,y)
\;-\;\mu(1-y)\pi(0,0)\,\mathcal{I}_2(x,y)\,\Bigr]
\end{equation}

In the same fasion of what was done for Model A using probabilistic arguments, we can try to take the perspective of a Class-$2$ customer to derive the marginal PGF of the non-priority class by means of probabilistic arguments. Allowing jockeying makes the assignment quite complicated, but a very similar argument can be derived if we set $\gamma_2=0$.

For $\gamma_2=0$, our fundamental equation reads
\begin{equation}
    \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) xy - \mu y - \lambda_1 x^2 y - \lambda_2 x y^2 \right] P(x,y) 
    + \gamma_1 xy(x-y) \frac{\partial}{\partial x} P(x,y) 
    = \mu \big[ (x-y) P_y(y) - x(1-y) \pi(0,0) \big].
    \label{eq:modelC_fundamental}
\end{equation}

To customers belonging to the non-priority class, given that the server is busy and that $N_1=0$, their effective service time is equivalent to the busy period ($BP$) of an $M/M/1+M$ queueing system with arrival rate $\lambda_1$, service rate $\mu$ and abandonment rate $\gamma_2$, with the very remarkable difference of those abandonments are not abandoning the system as such but joining to the non-priority queue.

The LST and expectation of such busy period are complicated, but known, expressions:
\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
    \widetilde{B}_C(s) = \frac{\mu}{\lambda_1 + \mu + s - \frac{\lambda_1(\mu + \gamma_1)}{\lambda_1 + \mu + \gamma_1 + s - \frac{\lambda_1(\mu + 2\gamma_1)}{\lambda_1 + \mu + 2\gamma_1 + s - \dots}}}.
    \label{eq:Bc_LST}
    \end{split}
    \begin{split}
        \mathbb{E}[B_C] = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{\lambda_1^{k-1}}{\prod_{i=1}^k (\mu + (i-1)\gamma_1)}.
    \label{eq:Bc_expectation}
    \end{split}
\end{align}

We again define $P_y(y)$ as follows:
\begin{equation}
    P_y(y) = \mathbb{P}(\text{busy}, N_1=0) \cdot \mathbb{E}[y^{N_2} \mid \text{busy}, N_1=0].
    \label{eq:pyy_prob_def_C}
\end{equation}

And apply the Pollaczek-Khinchine formula where the service times are $B_C$ and the arrival rate is $\lambda_2$. Again, we can say that
\begin{equation}
     \mathbb{E}[y^{N_2} \mid \text{busy}, N_1=0]=L_2(y)= \frac{(1 - \lambda_2 \mathbb{E}[B_C]) (1-y) \widetilde{B}_C(\lambda_2(1-y))}{\widetilde{B}_C(\lambda_2(1-y)) - y}.
    \label{eq:modelC_pk_formula}
\end{equation}

This leaves us with the following expression for $P_y(y)$:
\begin{equation}
    P_y(y) = \mathbb{P}(\text{busy}, N_1=0) \left[ \frac{(1 - \lambda_2 \mathbb{E}[B_C]) (1-y) \widetilde{B}_C(\lambda_2(1-y))}{\widetilde{B}_C(\lambda_2(1-y)) - y} \right].
    \label{eq:pyy_unsimplified}
\end{equation}

Evaluating that at $y=0$ allows us to simplify the result:
\begin{align}
    P_y(0) &= \pi(0,0) \nonumber \\
    &= \mathbb{P}(\text{busy}, N_1=0) \left[ \frac{(1 - \lambda_2 \mathbb{E}[B_C]) (1) \widetilde{B}_C(\lambda_2)}{\widetilde{B}_C(\lambda_2)} \right] \nonumber \\
    &= \mathbb{P}(\text{busy}, N_1=0) (1 - \lambda_2 \mathbb{E}[B_C]).
    \label{eq:py0_mapping}
\end{align}
Leaving us with
\begin{equation}
    P_y(y) = \pi(0,0) \left[ \frac{\widetilde{B}_C(\lambda_2(1-y))(1-y)}{\widetilde{B}_C(\lambda_2(1-y)) - y} \right].
    \label{eq:pyy_final_C}
\end{equation}

Allowing us to substitute the right-hand side of our fundamental equation for this variant of Model B where $\gamma_1=0$:
\begin{align*}
    \text{RHS} &= \mu \left[ (x-y) \left( \pi(0,0) \frac{\widetilde{B}_C(\lambda_2(1-y))(1-y)}{\widetilde{B}_C(\lambda_2(1-y)) - y} \right) - x(1-y) \pi(0,0) \right] \\
    &= \mu \pi(0,0) (1-y) \left[ \frac{(x-y)\widetilde{B}_C(\lambda_2(1-y))}{\widetilde{B}_C(\lambda_2(1-y)) - y} - x \right] \\
    &= \mu \pi(0,0) y(1-y) \left[ \frac{x - \widetilde{B}_C(\lambda_2(1-y))}{\widetilde{B}_C(\lambda_2(1-y)) - y} \right].
\end{align*}

In the end, we are left with
\begin{align}
    &\left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) xy - \mu y - \lambda_1 x^2 y - \lambda_2 x y^2 \right] P(x,y) 
    + \gamma_1 xy(x-y) \frac{\partial}{\partial x} P(x,y) \\
    & \quad = \quad \mu \pi(0,0) y(1-y) \left[ \frac{x - \widetilde{B}_C(\lambda_2(1-y))}{\widetilde{B}_C(\lambda_2(1-y)) - y} \right].
\end{align}

        & \quad = \quad \gamma_2 \sum_{n_1=1}^{\infty} \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} (n_2+1) \pi(n_1-1, n_2+1) x^{n_1} y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad = \quad \gamma_2 \sum_{n_1=1}^{\infty} \sum_{m_2=2}^{\infty} m_2 \pi(n_1-1, m_2) x^{n_1} y^{m_2-1} \\
        & \quad = \quad \gamma_2 x \left[
            \sum_{n_1=1}^{\infty} \sum_{m_2=0}^{\infty} m_2 \pi(n_1-1, m_2) x^{n_1-1} y^{m_2-1}
            - \sum_{n_1=1}^{\infty} \pi(n_1-1, 1) x^{n_1-1}
        \right] \\
        & \quad = \quad \gamma_2 x \left[
            \sum_{m_1=0}^{\infty} \sum_{m_2=0}^{\infty} m_2 \pi(m_1, m_2) x^{m_1} y^{m_2-1}
            - \sum_{m_1=0}^{\infty} \pi(m_1, 1) x^{m_1}
        \right] \\
        & \quad = \quad \gamma_2 x \left[
            \frac{\partial}{\partial y} P(x,y) - P_x(x | n_2=1)
        \right]
    \end{split} \\
    \begin{split}
        & RHS_6 \\
        & \quad = \quad \theta_1 \sum_{n_1=1}^{\infty} \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} (n_1+1) \pi(n_1+1, n_2) x^{n_1} y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad = \quad \theta_1 \sum_{m_1=2}^{\infty} \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} m_1 \pi(m_1, n_2) x^{m_1-1} y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad = \quad \theta_1 \left[
            \sum_{m_1=0}^{\infty} \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} m_1 \pi(m_1, n_2) x^{m_1-1} y^{n_2}
            - \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} \pi(1, n_2) y^{n_2}
        \right] \\
        & \quad = \quad \theta_1 \left[
            \sum_{m_1=0}^{\infty} \sum_{n_2=0}^{\infty} m_1 \pi(m_1, n_2) x^{m_1-1} y^{n_2}
            - \sum_{m_1=0}^{\infty} m_1 \pi(m_1, 0) x^{m_1-1}
        \right] \\
        & \quad \quad - \theta_1 \left[
            \sum_{n_2=0}^{\infty} \pi(1, n_2) y^{n_2}
            - \pi(1, 0)
        \right] \\
        & \quad = \quad \theta_1 \left[
            \frac{\partial}{\partial x} P(x,y) - \frac{d}{dx} P_x(x) - P_y(y | n_1=1) + \pi(1, 0)
        \right]
    \end{split} \\
    \begin{split}
        & RHS_7 \\
        & \quad = \quad \theta_2 \sum_{n_1=1}^{\infty} \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} (n_2+1) \pi(n_1, n_2+1) x^{n_1} y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad = \quad \theta_2 \sum_{n_1=1}^{\infty} \sum_{m_2=2}^{\infty} m_2 \pi(n_1, m_2) x^{n_1} y^{m_2-1} \\
        & \quad = \quad \theta_2 \left[
            \sum_{n_1=1}^{\infty} \sum_{m_2=0}^{\infty} m_2 \pi(n_1, m_2) x^{n_1} y^{m_2-1}
            - \sum_{n_1=1}^{\infty} \pi(n_1, 1) x^{n_1}
        \right] \\
        & \quad = \quad \theta_2 \left[
            \sum_{n_1=0}^{\infty} \sum_{m_2=0}^{\infty} m_2 \pi(n_1, m_2) x^{n_1} y^{m_2-1}
            - \sum_{m_2=0}^{\infty} m_2 \pi(0, m_2) y^{m_2-1}
        \right] \\
        & \quad \quad - \theta_2 \left[
            \sum_{n_1=0}^{\infty} \pi(n_1, 1) x^{n_1}
            - \pi(0, 1)
        \right] \\
        & \quad = \quad \theta_2 \left[
            \frac{\partial}{\partial y} P(x,y) - \frac{d}{dy} P_y(y) - P_x(x | n_2=1) + \pi(0, 1)
        \right]
    \end{split}
\end{align}

Now, we combine all terms, we group them and we multiply both sides by $xy$ in order to avoid negative exponents on $x$ and $y$, be it on that or subsequent equations:
\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
        & (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) \left[
            P(x,y) - P_y(y) - P_x(x) + \pi(0,0)
        \right] \\
        & + \gamma_1 x \left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} P(x,y) - \frac{d}{dx} P_x(x) \right] \\
        & + \gamma_2 y \left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial y} P(x,y) - \frac{d}{dy} P_y(y) \right] \\
        & + \theta_1 x \left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} P(x,y) - \frac{d}{dx} P_x(x) \right] \\
        & + \theta_2 y \left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial y} P(x,y) - \frac{d}{dy} P_y(y) \right] \\
        & \quad = \quad \mu x^{-1} \left[
            P(x,y) - x P_y(y | n_1=1) - P_y(y) - P_x(x) + x \pi(1,0) + \pi(0,0)
        \right] \\
        & \quad \quad + \lambda_1 x \left[ P(x,y) - P_x(x) \right] \\
        & \quad \quad + \lambda_2 y \left[ P(x,y) - P_y(y) \right] \\
        & \quad \quad + \gamma_1 y \left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} P(x,y) - P_y(y | n_1=1) \right] \\
        & \quad \quad + \gamma_2 x \left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial y} P(x,y) - P_x(x | n_2=1) \right] \\
        & \quad \quad + \theta_1 \left[
            \frac{\partial}{\partial x} P(x,y) - \frac{d}{dx} P_x(x) - P_y(y | n_1=1) + \pi(1, 0)
        \right] \\
        & \quad \quad + \theta_2 \left[
            \frac{\partial}{\partial y} P(x,y) - \frac{d}{dy} P_y(y) - P_x(x | n_2=1) + \pi(0, 1)
        \right]
    \end{split} \nonumber \\
    \begin{split}
        & \left[ (\lambda_1+\lambda_2+\mu) - \mu x^{-1} - \lambda_1 x - \lambda_2 y \right] P(x,y) \\
        & + \left[ \gamma_1(x-y) + \theta_1(x-1) \right] \frac{\partial}{\partial x} P(x,y) \\
        & + \left[ \gamma_2(y-x) + \theta_2(y-1) \right] \frac{\partial}{\partial y} P(x,y) \\
        & \quad = \quad \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) - \mu x^{-1} - \lambda_1 x \right] P_x(x) \\
        & \quad \quad + \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) - \mu x^{-1} - \lambda_2 y \right] P_y(y) \\
        & \quad \quad + \left[ -(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) + \mu x^{-1} \right] \pi(0,0) \\
        & \quad \quad + \left[ \gamma_1 x + \theta_1(x-1) \right] \frac{d}{dx} P_x(x) \\
        & \quad \quad + \left[ \gamma_2 y + \theta_2(y-1) \right] \frac{d}{dy} P_y(y) \\
        & \quad \quad - \left[ \mu + \gamma_1 y + \theta_1 \right] P_y(y | n_1=1) \\
        & \quad \quad - \left[ \gamma_2 x + \theta_2 \right] P_x(x | n_2=1) \\
        & \quad \quad + \mu \pi(1,0) \\
        & \quad \quad + \theta_1 \pi(1,0) \\
        & \quad \quad + \theta_2 \pi(0,1)
    \end{split} \nonumber \\
    \begin{split}
        & \left[ (\lambda_1+\lambda_2+\mu)xy - \mu y - \lambda_1 x^2 y - \lambda_2 x y^2 \right] P(x,y) \\
        & + xy \left[ \gamma_1(x-y) + \theta_1(x-1) \right] \frac{\partial}{\partial x} P(x,y) \\
        & + xy \left[ \gamma_2(y-x) + \theta_2(y-1) \right] \frac{\partial}{\partial y} P(x,y) \\
        & \quad = \quad \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu)xy - \mu y - \lambda_1 x^2 y \right] P_x(x) \\
        & \quad \quad + \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu)xy - \mu y - \lambda_2 x y^2 \right] P_y(y) \\
        & \quad \quad - \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu)xy - \mu y \right] \pi(0,0) \\
        & \quad \quad + xy \left[ \gamma_1 x + \theta_1(x-1) \right] \frac{d}{dx} P_x(x) \\
        & \quad \quad + xy \left[ \gamma_2 y + \theta_2(y-1) \right] \frac{d}{dy} P_y(y) \\
        & \quad \quad - xy \left[ \mu + \gamma_1 y + \theta_1 \right] P_y(y | n_1=1) \\
        & \quad \quad - xy \left[ \gamma_2 x + \theta_2 \right] P_x(x | n_2=1) \\
        & \quad \quad + (\mu + \theta_1) xy \, \pi(1,0) \\
        & \quad \quad + \theta_2 xy \, \pi(0,1)
    \end{split} \label{eq:gen_transform_interior_relation}
\end{align}

Now, we can recreate the equivalent steps for the $x$-boundary balance equation (Equation~\eqref{eq:gen_balance_x-boundary}):
\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
        & \sum_{n_1=1}^{\infty} \left[ \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu + (\gamma_1 + \theta_1) n_1 \right] \pi(n_1, 0) x^{n_1} \\
        & \quad = \quad \lambda_1 \sum_{n_1=1}^{\infty} \pi(n_1-1,0) x^{n_1}
        + \mu \sum_{n_1=1}^{\infty} \pi(n_1+1,0) x^{n_1} \\
        & \quad \quad + \gamma_2 \sum_{n_1=1}^{\infty} \pi(n_1-1, 1) x^{n_1}
        + \theta_1 \sum_{n_1=1}^{\infty} (n_1+1) \pi(n_1+1, 0) x^{n_1}
        + \theta_2 \sum_{n_1=1}^{\infty} \pi(n_1, 1) x^{n_1}
    \end{split} \nonumber \\
    \begin{split}
        & (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) \sum_{n_1=1}^{\infty} \pi(n_1, 0) x^{n_1}
        + (\gamma_1 + \theta_1) x \sum_{n_1=1}^{\infty} n_1 \pi(n_1, 0) x^{n_1-1} \\
        & \quad = \quad \lambda_1 x \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \pi(m, 0) x^{m}
        + \mu x^{-1} \sum_{m=2}^{\infty} \pi(m, 0) x^{m} \\
        & \quad \quad + \gamma_2 x \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \pi(m, 1) x^{m}
        + \theta_1 \sum_{m=2}^{\infty} m \pi(m, 0) x^{m-1}
        + \theta_2 \sum_{m=1}^{\infty} \pi(m, 1) x^{m}
    \end{split} \nonumber \\
    \begin{split}
        & (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) \left[ \sum_{n_1=0}^{\infty} \pi(n_1, 0) x^{n_1} - \pi(0,0) \right]
        + (\gamma_1 + \theta_1) x \sum_{n_1=0}^{\infty} n_1 \pi(n_1, 0) x^{n_1-1} \\
        & \quad = \quad \lambda_1 x \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \pi(m, 0) x^{m} \\
        & \quad \quad + \mu x^{-1} \left[ \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \pi(m, 0) x^{m} - x \pi(1, 0) - \pi(0,0) \right] \\
        & \quad \quad + \gamma_2 x \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \pi(m, 1) x^{m} \\
        & \quad \quad + \theta_1 \left[ \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} m \pi(m, 0) x^{m-1} - \pi(1, 0) \right] \\
        & \quad \quad + \theta_2 \left[ \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \pi(m, 1) x^{m} - \pi(0, 1) \right]
    \end{split} \nonumber \\
    \begin{split}
        & (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) \left[ P_x(x) - \pi(0,0) \right]
        + (\gamma_1 + \theta_1) x \frac{d}{dx} P_x(x) \\
        & \quad = \quad \lambda_1 x P_x(x)
        + \mu x^{-1} \left[ P_x(x) - x \pi(1, 0) - \pi(0, 0) \right] \\
        & \quad \quad + \gamma_2 x P_x(x | n_2=1)
        + \theta_1 \left[ \frac{d}{dx} P_x(x) - \pi(1, 0) \right]
        + \theta_2 \left[ P_x(x | n_2=1) - \pi(0, 1) \right]
    \end{split} \nonumber \\
    \begin{split}
        & \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) - \mu x^{-1} - \lambda_1 x \right] P_x(x)
        + \left[ \gamma_1 x + \theta_1(x-1) \right] \frac{d}{dx} P_x(x) \\
        & \quad = \quad \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) - \mu x^{-1} \right] \pi(0,0)
        - (\mu + \theta_1) \pi(1, 0)
        - \theta_2 \pi(0, 1) \\
        & \quad \quad + \left[ \gamma_2 x + \theta_2 \right] P_x(x | n_2=1)
    \end{split} \nonumber \\
    \begin{split}
        & \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu)xy - \mu y - \lambda_1 x^2 y \right] P_x(x)
        + xy \left[ \gamma_1 x + \theta_1(x-1) \right] \frac{d}{dx} P_x(x) \\
        & \quad = \quad \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu)xy - \mu y \right] \pi(0,0) \\
        & \quad \quad - (\mu + \theta_1) xy \, \pi(1, 0) \\
        & \quad \quad - \theta_2 xy \, \pi(0, 1) \\
        & \quad \quad + xy \left[ \gamma_2 x + \theta_2 \right] P_x(x | n_2=1)
    \end{split} \label{eq:gen_transform_x-boundary_relation}
\end{align}

Again, we replicate the procedure for the $y$-boundary balance equation (Equation~\eqref{eq:gen_balance_y-boundary}):
\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
        & \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} \left[ \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu + (\gamma_2 + \theta_2) n_2 \right] \pi(0, n_2) y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad = \quad \lambda_2 \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} \pi(0, n_2-1) y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad \quad + \mu \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} \pi(1, n_2) y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad \quad + \mu \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} \pi(0, n_2+1) y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad \quad + \gamma_1 \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} \pi(1, n_2-1) y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad \quad + \theta_1 \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} \pi(1, n_2) y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad \quad + \theta_2 \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} (n_2+1) \pi(0, n_2+1) y^{n_2}
    \end{split} \nonumber \\
    \begin{split}
        & (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} \pi(0, n_2) y^{n_2} \\
        & + (\gamma_2 + \theta_2) y \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} n_2 \pi(0, n_2) y^{n_2-1} \\
        & \quad = \quad \lambda_2 y \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \pi(0, m) y^{m} \\
        & \quad \quad + \mu \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} \pi(1, n_2) y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad \quad + \mu y^{-1} \sum_{m=2}^{\infty} \pi(0, m) y^{m} \\
        & \quad \quad + \gamma_1 y \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \pi(1, m) y^{m} \\
        & \quad \quad + \theta_1 \sum_{n_2=1}^{\infty} \pi(1, n_2) y^{n_2} \\
        & \quad \quad + \theta_2 \sum_{m=2}^{\infty} m \pi(0, m) y^{m-1}
    \end{split} \nonumber \\
    \begin{split}
        & (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) \left[ \sum_{n_2=0}^{\infty} \pi(0, n_2) y^{n_2} - \pi(0,0) \right] \\
        & + (\gamma_2 + \theta_2) y \sum_{n_2=0}^{\infty} n_2 \pi(0, n_2) y^{n_2-1} \\
        & \quad = \quad \lambda_2 y \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \pi(0, m) y^{m} \\
        & \quad \quad + \mu \left[ \sum_{n_2=0}^{\infty} \pi(1, n_2) y^{n_2} - \pi(1, 0) \right] \\
        & \quad \quad + \mu y^{-1} \left[ \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \pi(0, m) y^{m} - y \pi(0, 1) - \pi(0, 0) \right] \\
        & \quad \quad + \gamma_1 y \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \pi(1, m) y^{m} \\
        & \quad \quad + \theta_1 \left[ \sum_{n_2=0}^{\infty} \pi(1, n_2) y^{n_2} - \pi(1, 0) \right] \\
        & \quad \quad + \theta_2 \left[ \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} m \pi(0, m) y^{m-1} - \pi(0, 1) \right]
    \end{split} \nonumber \\
    \begin{split}
        & (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) \left[ P_y(y) - \pi(0, 0) \right]
        + (\gamma_2 + \theta_2) y \frac{d}{dy} P_y(y) \\
        & \quad = \quad \lambda_2 y P_y(y)
        + \mu \left[ P_y(y | n_1=1) - \pi(1, 0) \right] \\
        & \quad \quad + \mu y^{-1} \left[ P_y(y) - y \pi(0, 1) - \pi(0, 0) \right]
        + \gamma_1 y P_y(y | n_1=1) \\
        & \quad \quad + \theta_1 \left[ P_y(y | n_1=1) - \pi(1, 0) \right]
        + \theta_2 \left[ \frac{d}{dy} P_y(y) - \pi(0, 1) \right]
    \end{split} \nonumber \\
    \begin{split}
        & \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) - \mu y^{-1} - \lambda_2 y \right] P_y(y)
        + \left[ \gamma_2 y + \theta_2(y-1) \right] \frac{d}{dy} P_y(y) \\
        & \quad = \quad \left[ \mu + \gamma_1 y + \theta_1 \right] P_y(y | n_1=1) \\
        & \quad \quad - (\mu + \theta_1) \pi(1, 0)
        - (\mu + \theta_2) \pi(0, 1) \\
        & \quad \quad + \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) - \mu y^{-1} \right] \pi(0, 0)
    \end{split} \nonumber \\
    \begin{split}
        & \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) - \mu y^{-1} - \lambda_2 y \right] P_y(y)
        + \left[ \gamma_2 y + \theta_2(y-1) \right] \frac{d}{dy} P_y(y) \\
        & \quad = \quad \left[ \mu + \gamma_1 y + \theta_1 \right] P_y(y | n_1=1) \\
        & \quad \quad + \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) - \mu y^{-1} - (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2) \right] \pi(0, 0)
    \end{split} \nonumber \\
    \begin{split}
        & \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu) - \mu y^{-1} - \lambda_2 y \right] P_y(y)
        + \left[ \gamma_2 y + \theta_2(y-1) \right] \frac{d}{dy} P_y(y) \\
        & \quad = \quad \left[ \mu + \gamma_1 y + \theta_1 \right] P_y(y | n_1=1)
        + \left[ \mu - \mu y^{-1} \right] \pi(0, 0)
    \end{split} \nonumber \\
    \begin{split}
        & \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu)xy - \mu x - \lambda_2 x y^2 \right] P_y(y)
        + xy \left[ \gamma_2 y + \theta_2(y-1) \right] \frac{d}{dy} P_y(y) \\
        & \quad = \quad \left[ \mu + \gamma_1 y + \theta_1 \right] xy \, P_y(y | n_1=1)
        - \mu x(1-y) \, \pi(0,0)
    \end{split} \label{eq:gen_transform_y-boundary_relation}
\end{align}

We can now combine Equation~\eqref{eq:gen_transform_interior_relation} and Equation~\eqref{eq:gen_transform_x-boundary_relation}, where many terms cancel out, leaving us with the following, remarkably cleaner, expression:
\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
        & \left[ (\lambda_1+\lambda_2+\mu)xy - \mu y - \lambda_1 x^2 y - \lambda_2 x y^2 \right] P(x,y) \\
        & + xy \left[ \gamma_1(x-y) + \theta_1(x-1) \right] \frac{\partial}{\partial x} P(x,y) \\
        & + xy \left[ \gamma_2(y-x) + \theta_2(y-1) \right] \frac{\partial}{\partial y} P(x,y) \\
        & \quad = \quad \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu)xy - \mu y - \lambda_2 x y^2 \right] P_y(y) \\
        & \quad \quad + xy \left[ \gamma_2 y + \theta_2(y-1) \right] \frac{d}{dy} P_y(y) \\
        & \quad \quad - xy \left[ \mu + \gamma_1 y + \theta_1 \right] P_y(y | n_1=1)
    \end{split} \label{eq:gen_plugged_interior_and_x}
\end{align}

Now, isolating $\left[ \mu + \gamma_1 y + \theta_1 \right] xy \, P_y(y | n_1=1)$ in Equation~\eqref{eq:gen_transform_y-boundary_relation} and plugging it into Equation~\eqref{eq:gen_plugged_interior_and_x} makes a cascade of cancelations that result in:
\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
        & \left[ (\lambda_1+\lambda_2+\mu)xy - \mu y - \lambda_1 x^2 y - \lambda_2 x y^2 \right] P(x,y) \\
        & + xy \left[ \gamma_1(x-y) + \theta_1(x-1) \right] \frac{\partial}{\partial x} P(x,y) \\
        & + xy \left[ \gamma_2(y-x) + \theta_2(y-1) \right] \frac{\partial}{\partial y} P(x,y) \\
        & \quad = \quad \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu)xy - \mu y - \lambda_2 x y^2 \right] P_y(y) \\
        & \quad \quad + xy \left[ \gamma_2 y + \theta_2(y-1) \right] \frac{d}{dy} P_y(y) \\
        & \quad \quad - \left\{ \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu)xy - \mu x - \lambda_2 x y^2 \right] P_y(y) \right. \\
        & \quad \quad \quad \left. + xy \left[ \gamma_2 y + \theta_2(y-1) \right] \frac{d}{dy} P_y(y) + \mu x(1-y) \, \pi(0,0) \right\}
    \end{split} \nonumber \\
    \begin{split}
        & \left[ (\lambda_1+\lambda_2+\mu)xy - \mu y - \lambda_1 x^2 y - \lambda_2 x y^2 \right] P(x,y) \\
        & + xy \left[ \gamma_1(x-y) + \theta_1(x-1) \right] \frac{\partial}{\partial x} P(x,y) \\
        & + xy \left[ \gamma_2(y-x) + \theta_2(y-1) \right] \frac{\partial}{\partial y} P(x,y) \\
        & \quad = \quad \mu(x-y) P_y(y) - \mu x(1-y) \, \pi(0,0)
    \end{split} \label{eq:gen_fundamental_equation}.
\end{align}

\subsection{Model A: Probabilistic approach}

The approach here is less direct, but probably more insightful and rich in terms of arguments. Here we will look at the system in states $(0,n_2), n_2 \geq 0$, where the priority queue is empty and the server is busy. The main idea is that, if we find the system in that state and there is a class-$1$ arrival, the server will start a period of "vacation" where the server will serve that priority customer first and an undetermined number of priority class arrivals that may arrive before this new priority arrival's service is completed. Such a reasoning will help us provide an expression for $P_y(y)$ and, ultimately, one for $P(x,y)$. We will now define the following random variables:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $L$: the number of (class-$2$) customers in the system
    \item $L_Q$: the number of (class-$2$) customers in the queue
\end{itemize}

\begin{proof}

    Let service times of an $M/G/1$ queue be given by the random variable $B$. The Laplace-Stieltjes Transform of the number of customers in the system  -i.e. $L(z)$ is given by the Pollazcek-Khinchine formula:
    \begin{equation}
        L(x)
        = \frac{
            (1 - \mathbb{E}[B]) (1-z) \widetilde{B}(\lambda_2(1-z))
        }{
            \widetilde{B}(\lambda_2(1-z) - z
        }.
        \label{eq:pk_Lz}
    \end{equation}

    By conditioning on the class-$2$ subsystem being busy (i.e. $L>0$), we can obtain the following relation:
    \begin{align*}
        L(z)
        &= \mathbb{E}\left[ z^{L} \right] \\
        &= \mathbb{E}[z^L |L=0] \mathbb{P}(L=0) + \mathbb{E}[z^L |L>0] \mathbb{P}(L>0) \\
        &= (1 - \lambda_2 \mathbb{E}[B]) + \lambda_2 \mathbb{E}[B] \cdot \mathbb{E}[z^{L} | \text{L>0}] \\
        &= (1 - \lambda_2 \mathbb{E}[B]) + \lambda_2 \mathbb{E}[B] \cdot \mathbb{E}[z^{1 +L_Q} | \text{L>0}] \\
        &= (1 - \lambda_2 \mathbb{E}[B]) + \lambda_2 \mathbb{E}[B] \cdot z\mathbb{E}[z^{L_Q} | \text{L>0}]
    \end{align*}
    Rearranging the expression yields
    \begin{equation}
        \mathbb{E}[z^{L_Q} | L>0] = \frac{
            \mathbb{E}[z^L] - (1 - \lambda_2 \mathbb{E}[B])
        }{
            z \cdot \lambda_2 \mathbb{E}[B]
        }
    \end{equation}
    By plugging equation \eqref{eq:pk_Lz} in, we obtain
    \begin{align*}
        \mathbb{E}[z^{L_Q} | L>0]
        &= \frac{
            (1 - \lambda_2\mathbb{E}[B]) (1-z) \widetilde{B}(\lambda_2(1-z)) - (1 - \lambda_2 \mathbb{E}[B])
        }{
           z \lambda_1\mathbb{E}[B] (\widetilde{B}(\lambda_2(1-z)) - z)
        } \\
        &= \frac{
            1 - \lambda_2 \mathbb{E}[B]
        }{
            z \lambda_1\mathbb{E}[B]
        } \cdot \frac{
            (1 -z) \widetilde{B}(\lambda_2(1-z)) - 1
        }{
            \widetilde{B}(\lambda_2(1-z)) - z
        } \\
        &= \frac{
            1 - \lambda_2 \mathbb{E}[B]
        }{
            z \lambda_1\mathbb{E}[B]
        } \cdot \frac{
            (1 -z) \widetilde{B}(\lambda_2(1-z)) -  \widetilde{B}(\lambda_2(1-z)) + z
        }{
            \widetilde{B}(\lambda_2(1-z)) - z
        } \\
        &= \frac{
            1 - \lambda_2 \mathbb{E}[B]
        }{
            z \lambda_1\mathbb{E}[B]
        } \cdot \frac{
            z - z \widetilde{B}(\lambda_2(1-z)) 
        }{
            \widetilde{B}(\lambda_2(1-z)) - z
        } \\
        &= \frac{
            1 - \lambda_2 \mathbb{E}[B]
        }{
            \lambda_1\mathbb{E}[B]
        } \cdot \frac{
            1 - \widetilde{B}(\lambda_2(1-z)) 
        }{
            \widetilde{B}(\lambda_2(1-z)) - z
        }.
    \end{align*}

    When only looking at class-$2$ customers in states where the service is busy and the priority queue is empty —i.e. states $(0,n_2), n_2 \geq 0$, we find that it is equivalent to a $M/G/1$ queue with Poisson arrivals at rate $\lambda_2$ and general service times $B$ distributed as the busy period of a queue $M/M/1$ with arrival rate $\lambda_1$ and exponential service times with rate $\mu$. The LST and the expectation for the busy period of such system are in Equations \eqref{eq:bp_mm1} and \eqref{eq:bp_expected_mm1}, respectively. Plugging in our constants yields
    \begin{align}
        \widetilde{B}(\lambda_2(1-z)) 
        &= \frac{
            (\mu + \lambda_1 + \lambda_2(1-z))
            - \sqrt{
                (\mu + \lambda_1 + \lambda_2(1-z))^2 - 4 \lambda_1
            }
        }{
            2 \lambda_1
        }         \\
        \lambda_2 \mathbb{E}[B] &= \frac{\rho_2}{1 - \rho_1}.
    \end{align}
    With $\frac{1 - \lambda_2 \mathbb{E}[B]}{\lambda_2 \mathbb{E}[B]} =  \frac{1 - \rho}{\rho_2}$, we can immediately derive that
    \begin{equation*}
        \mathbb{E}[z^{L_Q} | L>0]
        = \frac{1 - \rho}{\rho_2}
        \cdot
        \frac{1 -\widetilde{B}(\lambda_2 (1-z))}{\widetilde{B}(\lambda_2 (1-z)) - z}
    \end{equation*}
    In order to align this result with the one obtained analytically in the previous subsection, note that $\widetilde{B}(\lambda_2(1-z)) = x^*(y)$, so we can rewrite the expresson above:
    \begin{equation*}
        \mathbb{E}[z^{L_Q} | L>0]
        = \frac{1 - \rho}{\rho_2}
        \cdot
        \frac{1 -x^*(y)}{x^*(y) - z}.
    \end{equation*}



\end{proof}











\section{ DUMPSTER SECTION: $M/M/1$ queue with $2$ priority classes}

In order to address a more complex system with abandonment rates in each queue or with customers with impatience, such as the one studied using approximation-based methods in \cite{de1992approximate}, it is reasonable to study a simpler model, such as the one that will follow in this section. The idea is that the techniques and methods employed in this section will serve as a foundation for the analysis of the models in which we are more interested.

\subsection{Marginal PGF of the priority class}

To examine the marginal PGF of the priority class, it suffices to plug $y=1$ into Equation \eqref{eq:P_x}, as $P(x,y)=\mathbb{E}\left[ x^{n_1}y^{n_2} \right]$, $P(x,1)=\mathbb{E}\left[ x^{n_1}1^{n_2} \right] =\mathbb{E}\left[ x^{n_1} \right]$. We would expect this to behave as a single class $M/M/1$-queue with arrival rate $\lambda_1$ and service rate $\mu$. As described in many manuals, including \cite{adan2002queueing}, this should be geometrically distributed with parameter $\rho_1 = \frac{\lambda_1}{\mu}$.
\begin{align}
    \left[ -\lambda_1 x^2 + \lambda_1 x + \mu x - \mu \right] P(x,1)
    &= \mu (x-1) P_y(1) \\
     \left[ (x-1) (\mu - \lambda_1 x) \right] P(x,1) 
     &= \mu (x-1) P_y(1) \\
      (\mu - \lambda_1 x)P(x,1) 
      &= \mu P_y(1) \\\
      \left[ 1-\rho_1 x \right] P(x,1)
      &= P_y(1) \\
      P(x,1)
      &= \frac{P_y(1)}{1 - \rho_1 x}.
\end{align}
We can use  $P(1,1)=\sum_{n_1=0}^{\infty} \pi(n_1,n_2) = 1 - \pi_0 = 1-(1-\rho)=\rho$ in the above relation evaluated at $x=1$ to obtain $P_y(1)= \rho (1 - \rho_1)$. Thus, the marginal PGF of the number of customers from the priority class waiting in the queue is
\begin{equation}
    P(x,1) = \rho \frac{1-\rho_1}{1-\rho_1x}. \label{eq:pgf_marginal_x}
\end{equation}
This result aligns with what is known for a classic single class $M/M/1$-queue with arrival rate $\lambda_1$ and service rate $\mu$, where the PGF of the number of customers in the system is $P(x)=\frac{1-\rho_1}{1 - \rho_1 x}$. The presence of $\rho=\frac{\lambda_1+\lambda_2}{\mu}$ in Equation \eqref{eq:pgf_marginal_x}here is motivated by the fact that we are looking at the number of people in the queue and conditioning on the fact that the server is busy, so we still need to account for the class-$2$ arrivals when the server is idle.

\subsection{Derivation total number of customers}

Another sanity check that can be done is looking at the total number of customers in the queues by substituting $y=x$ into Equation \eqref{eq:Pxy}. This follows from the fact that $P(x,x) = \mathbb{E}[x^{N_1}x^{N_2}] = \mathbb{E}[x^{N_1+N_2}]$. This should behave like an $M/M/1$-queue with arrival rate $\lambda = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2$ and service rate $\mu$.
\begin{align*}
    \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu)x^2 - \mu x -\lambda_1 x^3 - \lambda_2 x^3 \right] P(x,x) 
    & = - \mu x (x-1) \pi(0,0) \\
      \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \mu)x - \mu -\lambda_1 x^2 - \lambda_2 x^2 \right] P(x,x)
    & = - \mu (x-1) \pi(0,0) \\
    \left[ (x-1) \left[ x- \frac{\mu}{\lambda_1 + \lambda_2} \right]  \right] P(x,x) 
    & = - \mu (x-1) \pi(0,0) \\
    P(x,x)
    & = \frac{\pi(0,0)}{\frac{\mu}{\lambda_1 + \lambda_2} - x} \\
    P(x,x)
    & = \frac{\pi(0,0)}{1 - x\rho}.
\end{align*}
If we evaluate the above equation at $x=1$, we will be able to use $P(1,1)=1-\pi_0 = \rho$ to obtain $\pi(0,0)=\rho(1-\rho)$. Therefore, we have
\begin{equation}
    P(x,x) = \rho \frac{(1-\rho)}{1 - x\rho}.
\end{equation}
This, again, aligns with the PGF for the total number of customers in the system for the $M/M/1$-queue with arrival rate $\lambda$ and service rate $\mu$, but with the presence of a $\rho$ term in front of it, as we are once more conditioning on the fact that the server is busy in order to account only for the people in the queues.



\subsection{Finding $P_y(y)$}

As Equation \eqref{eq:Pxy} involves a term with the $y$-boundary function $P_y(y)$, we derive an expression for $P_y(y)$. To do so, we observe the system only in the epochs of states $(0, n_2)$ for $n_2 \geq 0$, i.e. when the server is busy, the priority queue is empty and there are $n_2$ class-$2$ customers in the non-priority queue.

Only looking at those moments in time where these conditions are met allows us to see how the system behaves in the $y$-boundary, where the service times between two valid states will no longer be exponentially distributed.

The non-priority queue, when only observed at states $(0,n_2)$ for $n_2 \geq 0$, behaves like a $M/G/1$-queue with arrival rate $\lambda_2$ and service time distribution equal to the busy period ($BP$) of a $M/M/1$-queue with arrival rate $\lambda_1$ and service rate $\mu$. The intuition behind this is that, when we leave state $(0,n_2)$, class-$2$ customers will have to wait until class-$1$ queue is empty again in order to be served.

In \cite{tian2006vacation}, the authors study the so-called Multiple Vacation Models. A result is given for the distribution of the number of customers in the queue ($L_v$), where it behaves as the sum of two independent random variables $L$ and $L_d$, where $L$ refers to that of the classic models $M/G/1$ and $L_d$ accounts for the delay caused by the vacation effect. It reads like this:
\begin{equation*}
    L_v = L+ L_d.
\end{equation*}
Naturally, the PGF of that is the following:
\begin{equation}
    L_v(z) = \mathbb{E}\left[ z^{L_v} \right]
    = \mathbb{E}\left[ z^{L + L_d} \right]
    = \mathbb{E}\left[ z^{L} \right] \mathbb{E}\left[ z^{L_d} \right] = L(z) L_d(z).
\end{equation}

In their book, they provide closed formulas for both expressions, being as follows:
\begin{align*}
    L(z) = \frac{(1-\rho) (1-z) \widetilde{B}(\lambda(1-z))}{\widetilde{B}(\lambda(1-z)) - z}
     \quad \text{and} \quad
     L_d(z) = \frac{1 - \widetilde{B}(\lambda(1-z))}{ \lambda_2\mathbb{E}\left[V\right] (1-z)},
\end{align*}
where V is the random variable for the total vacation time taken by the servers. In our case, this is simply the busy period of the $M/M/1$-queue with arrival rate $\lambda_1$ and service rate $\mu$. It is a known result that for such model we have $\mathbb{E} \left[ BP \right] = \frac{1}{\mu - \lambda_1}$. With this information we have the following relation:
\begin{align*}
    L_v(z) 
    &= \frac{(1-\rho_2) (1-z) \widetilde{B}(\lambda_2(1-z))}{\widetilde{B}(\lambda_2(1-z)) - z}
    \frac{1 - \widetilde{B}(\lambda_2(1-z))}{\frac{1}{\mu - \lambda_1} (1-z)} \\
    &= \frac{(1-\rho_2) \widetilde{B}(\lambda_2(1-z))}{\widetilde{B}(\lambda_2(1-z)) - z}
    \frac{1 - \widetilde{B}(\lambda_2(1-z))}{\lambda_2\frac{1}{\mu - \lambda_1}}.
\end{align*}

Now we have that $B$ for our $M/G/1$-queue, conditional on the server being busy, corresponds to the busy period of an $M/M/1$-queue with arrival rate $\lambda_1$ and service rate $\mu$. This is a very well known result, and it is explained in a very clear manner by \cite{adan2002queueing}. They show that
\begin{equation}
   \widetilde{B}(s) = \widetilde{BP}_1(s) = \frac{
   ( \mu + \lambda_1 + s) - \sqrt{( \mu + \lambda_1 + s)^2-4\lambda_1 \mu}
    }{2\lambda_1},
\end{equation}
which for $s= \lambda_2 (1-z)$ is
\begin{equation}
    \widetilde{B}(\lambda_2 (1-z)) = \widetilde{BP}_1(\lambda_2 (1-z)) = \frac{
   ( \mu + \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 (1-z)) - \sqrt{( \mu + \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 (1-z))^2-4\lambda_1 \mu}
    }{2\lambda_1}, \label{eq:bp_mm1_local}
\end{equation}

\begin{lemma}[Queue-length decomposition for Multiple Adaptive Vacation models]
Consider an $M/G/1$-queue with Poisson arrival rate $\lambda$, i.i.d generally distributed service times $B$ where the server is expected to take multiple vacations $V$. 
Let the number of customers in the system be
\begin{equation}
    L_v = L + L_d,
\end{equation}
where the term $L$ corresponds to the number of customers in the system of such an $M/G/1$-queue without vacations and the term $L_d$ captures the increase caused by the multiple adaptive vacation effect.
The Laplace-Stiltjes Transform for $L_v$ is given by
\begin{equation}
    L_v(z) = \mathbb{E}\left[ z^{L_v} \right]
    = \mathbb{E}\left[ z^{L + L_d} \right]
    = \mathbb{E}\left[ z^{L} \right] \mathbb{E}\left[ z^{L_d} \right] = L(z) L_d(z),
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
    L(z) = \frac{(1-\rho) (1-z) \widetilde{B}(\lambda(1-z))}{\widetilde{B}(\lambda(1-z)) - z}
    \quad \text{and} \quad
    L_d(z) = \frac{1 - \widetilde{B}(\lambda(1-z))}{ \lambda_2\mathbb{E}\left[V\right] (1-z)}.
\end{equation}.
Thus, the LST for the Multiple Adaptive Vacation model for such a queue is given by
\begin{equation}
    L_v(z)
    = \frac{(1-\rho) \widetilde{B}(\lambda(1-z))}{\widetilde{B}(\lambda(1-z)) - z}
    \frac{1 - \widetilde{B}(\lambda(1-z))}{ \lambda_2\mathbb{E}\left[V\right]}.
\end{equation}
\label{lem:mav}
\end{lemma}
\bigskip

Applying Lemma \ref{lem:mav} to our problem, we can consider the $y$-boundary $P_y(y)$ as a $M/G/1$-queue with Poisson arrival rate $\lambda_2$ and service rate equal to the busy period of the $M/M/1$-queue with arrival rate $\lambda_1$ and service rate $\mu$, which we will denote by $\widetilde{BP}_1$.

In order to apply the Lemma \ref{lem:mav} to our particular problem of finding an expression for the $y$-boundary term, we must again consider our $M/G/1$-queue with the Poisson arrival rate $\lambda_2$ and with service times that are distributed as the busy period of a $M/M/1$-queue with arrival rate $\lambda_1$ and service rate $\mu$, which we will denote by $BP_{M/M/1}$. In the result of the lemma, we must carry out the following substitutions: $\lambda=\lambda_2$, $\rho=\rho_2$, $\widetilde{B}=\widetilde{BP}_1$ and $\mathbb{E}\left[V\right] = \mathbb{E}\left[\widetilde{BP}_1\right]$. Applying these changes leaves us with the following result:
\begin{equation}
    L_v(z)
    = \frac{(1-\rho_2) \widetilde{BP}_1(\lambda_2(1-z))}{\widetilde{BP}_1(\lambda_2(1-z)) - z}
    \frac{1 - \widetilde{BP}_1(\lambda_2(1-z))}{ \lambda_2\mathbb{E}\left[BP_{M/M/1}\right]}.
\end{equation}

The results for $\widetilde{BP}_1(s)$ and $\mathbb{E}\left[BP_{M/M/1}\right]$ of a generic $M/M/1$-queue can be found in many sources, including \cite{adan2002queueing}:
\begin{align*}
    \widetilde{BP}_1(s) = \frac{
   ( \mu + \lambda_1 + s) - \sqrt{( \mu + \lambda_1 + s)^2-4\lambda_1 \mu}
    }{2\lambda_1}
    \quad \text{and } \quad
    \mathbb{E}\left[BP_{M/M/1}\right] = \frac{1/ \mu}{1 - \rho_1}.
\end{align*}
Applied to our case, note that the arrival rate is $\lambda_1$ and that we need to substitute $s=\lambda_2(1-z)$. So the expressions read as follows
\begin{align}
    \widetilde{BP}_1\!\left[\lambda_2(1-z)\right]
    &=
    \frac{
  (\mu + \lambda_1 + \lambda_2(1-z))
    - \sqrt{(\mu + \lambda_1 + \lambda_2(1-z))^2 - 4\lambda_1 \mu}
    }{
    2\lambda_1
    },
    \label{eq:bp_mm1}\\
    \mathbb{E}\!\left[BP_{M/M/1}\right]
    &= \frac{1/\mu}{1 - \rho_1}
    \label{eq:ebp_mm1}.
\end{align}

Now, combining these results allows us to find an expression for $P_y(y)$:
\begin{align*}
    L_v(z) 
    &=  \frac{
            (1-\rho_2)\widetilde{BP}_1
        }{
            \widetilde{BP}_1  - z
        }
        \cdot
        \frac{1 - \widetilde{BP}_1 }{\lambda_2\frac{1/\mu}{1 - \rho_1}} \\
    &=  \frac{
            (1 - \rho_1)(1 - \rho_2)
        }{
            \rho_2
        } 
        \cdot
        \frac{
            \widetilde{BP}_1(1 - \widetilde{BP}_1)
        }{
            \widetilde{BP}_1-z
        } \\
    &=  \frac{(1 - \rho_1)(1 - \rho_2)}{ \rho_2} 
        \cdot
        \frac{
            \widetilde{BP}_1 \left[1 - \widetilde{BP}_1(\lambda_2(1-z))\right]
        }{
            \widetilde{BP}_1 - z
        },
\end{align*}
with $\widetilde{BP}_1(\lambda_2(1-z)$ defined as in Equation \eqref{eq:bp_mm1}.

\begin{theorem}[Probability Generating Function for the $M/M/1$-queue with $2$ priority classes and common service rate]
    
    Consider a queuing system with $2$ priority classes with Poisson arrival rates $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$, where class-$1$ customers have non-preemptive priority over those of class-$2$, and exponentially distributed service times with rate $\mu$ independently of the class.
    
    The Probability Generating Function $P(x,y)$ of such a system is given by
    
    \begin{eqnarray*}
        & & \hspace{-3em} \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 +\mu) xy - \mu y - \lambda_1 x^2 y - \lambda_2 xy^2 \right]  P(x,y) \\
        &=& \;      \mu (x-y) P_y(y) \\
        & & + \;  \mu x (y-1) \pi(0,0) \\
        & & \hspace{-3em} \left[ (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 +\mu) xy - \mu y - \lambda_1 x^2 y - \lambda_2 xy^2 \right]  P(x,y) \\
        &=& \; \mu (x-y) \frac{(1 - \rho_1)(1 - \rho_2)}{ \rho_2} 
        \cdot
        \frac{
            \widetilde{BP}_1(\lambda_2(1-y)) \left[1 - \widetilde{BP}_1(\lambda_2(1-y))\right]
        }{
            \widetilde{BP}_1(\lambda_2(1-y)) - y
        } \\
        & & - \mu x (y-1) \pi(0,0) \; 
    \end{eqnarray*}
\end{theorem}
