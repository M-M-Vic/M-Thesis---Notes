\begin{abstract}
We study the stationary queue-length distribution of a two-class non-preemptive priority $M/M/1$ queue extended with two customer-departure mechanisms: \emph{jockeying}, in which a waiting customer switches queues at a per-customer rate (conserving the total population), and \emph{abandonment}, in which a waiting customer leaves the system entirely (reducing the total population). Combining both mechanisms in full generality yields an intractable model (Model~X); we instead analyse a hierarchy of tractable specializations---Model~A (baseline), Model~B (bidirectional jockeying, intentionally left open), Model~B$_2$ (one-way jockeying $1\to2$), and Model~C$_2$ (class-1 abandonment)---and derive exact stationary solutions for Models~A, B$_2$, and C$_2$.

The analysis is organized around a bivariate probability generating function (PGF) $P(x,y)$ derived from the balance equations. Model~A reduces to an algebraic equation solved by the kernel method; Models~B$_2$ and~C$_2$ reduce to first-order linear ODEs in $x$, each requiring an integrating factor whose singularity structure pins the boundary function $P_y(y)$---via an analyticity condition at an interior point for B$_2$ and a boundedness condition at the domain boundary for C$_2$. In both cases the solution closes in terms of the confluent hypergeometric function $_1F_1$, reflecting an underlying Erlang-A structure shared by the two models.

A comparative analysis shows that jockeying preserves all total-population statistics (identical $\pi_0$, $\pi(0,0)$, and $\mathbb{E}[N]$ to Model~A) while redistrib\-uting load between classes, whereas abandonment strictly weakens the stability condition and reduces all queue lengths. As an extension we introduce and solve two head-of-line variants (Models~B$_2^1$ and~C$_2^1$) in which only the first waiting customer is subject to the departure mechanism; these admit rational closed forms via the kernel method. All results are validated numerically against the exact stationary distribution of a truncated continuous-time Markov chain.
\end{abstract}
